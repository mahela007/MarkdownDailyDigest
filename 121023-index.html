Ileal absorption

#2020BSQ-JUL/Q07

  • The glucose and amino acids in food have largely been absorbed by the time food makes it to the ileum. However, a significant amount of long chain fatty acids are absorbed in the ileum.
  • Bile salts are reabsorbed in the ileum.
    • Bile salt reabsorption is required for absorption of fats and lipids because the liver cannot synthesize new bile salts fast enough to replace losses. Ileal reabsorption is active. Therefore, with ileal resection, there is defective fat absorption.
    • Subsequently there is decreased absorption of fat soluble vitamin (A,D,E,K)
    • When unabsorbed bile salts enter the colon, they promote chloride secretion and cause diarrhoea.
    • [[BileSaltsAndPigments.png]] <- open this!
  • Most vitamins are absorbed in the proximal small intestine but absorption of vitamin B12, complexed to intrinsic factor, occurs in the small intestine.
  • The ileum is 'relatively sterile' compared to the colon and colonic reflux is prevented by the ileocoecal valve.
  • Hypocalcemia is a possible complication of intestinal resection (Ganong's - clinical box 24-6)

[!NOTE] What is MIC?
"MIC is the lowest concentration of an antibacterial agent expressed in mg/L (μg/mL) which, under strictly controlled in vitro conditions, completely prevents visible growth of the test strain of an organism [2]" Source

Post antibiotic effect

[!INFO] Post antibiotic effect
This is the persistence of antibiotic effect observed long after the serum concentration has fallen below the MIC.

Drugs with significant post antibiotic effects

  • Mostly seen in antibiotics which have concentration dependent kill characteristics.
  • Macrolides do have a strong post antibiotic effect but they are time-dependent killing antibiotics.
  • They benefit from large, intermittent doses.
  • Includes the following

[WARNING!] Macrolides don't have a true post antibiotic effect
They only appear to have a post antibiotic effect because they are sequestered within macrophages! Therefore, they are classified as having a post antibiotic effect above but they are actually time dependent ABxs

Drugs with weak or no post antibiotic effect

These act during some critical point in the bacterial cell cycle (like cell division). Therefore, the drug should be above MIC for a significant period of time to increase the odds that most of the bacteria will arrive at this point in the life cycle while the drug is above MIC.

Time dependent killing

Source

Think of these as having a
The antibiotics most affected by this are:

  • Those which kill bacteria most effectively at some specific event which occurs intermittently, eg. classically when the bacteria are about to divide.
  • Those which do not have much of a post-antibiotic effect, i.e. the killing only occurs while the drug concentration remains high

Examples of this include β-lactams, carbapenems, monobactams, linezolid, linocosamides like clindamycin, and macrolides like erythromycin.
This doesn't mean that concentration has to be > MIC for 100% of the dosing interval.
For these antibiotics, the required time spent above MIC increases if the bacteria are in a site to which antibiotic penetration is limited.

Concentration dependent killing

Seen with antibiotic which disable some crucial step in bacterial metabolism.
The higher the concentration, the more bacterial are disabled.

for aminoglycosides to obtain a clinical response of around 90%, the peak level needs to exceed the MIC by eight to ten times.

Stimuli for glucagon secretion:

  • Glucagon is secreted into the portal vein so that there is significant metabolism in the liver before it reaches the systemic circulation. Systemic levels are low.
  • Overall, it's secreted when the body needs more glucose but also when there is incoming food -i.e amino acids, CCK and gastrin (in the table)

Effects of glucagon:

[!INFO] Mainly catabolism of stores to increase blood glucose, except in muscle.

It acts on a GPCR with somewhat organ specific effects.

  1. liver
    • Increases glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis (from amino acids) and lypolysis (and thus increased kegenesis) - ( all mediated by ⬆cAMP)
  2. Muscle:
    • In muscle, glucagon does not cause glycogenolysis.
  3. Heart
    • Exogenous, large doses increases inotropy by increasing myocyte cAMP.
  4. Stimulates release of other hormones:
    1. growth hormone, insulin and pancreatic somatostatin secretion is increased.

  • Catecholamines increase hepatic glucose output;
  • Epinephrine (adrenaline) decreases peripheral uptake of glucose.
    • 📑provides anti-insulin action.
    • 📑This activity is important in type 1 diabetics because they lose the ability to secrete glucagon as well! (protects them against hypoglycaemia)
  • Thyroxine: hyperthyroidism exacerbates diabetes. Thyroxine increases gut absorption of glucose and potentiate catecholamine action (which is as above)
  • Glucocorticoids: Have a permissive effect on the actions of glucagon and attenuating action on insulin.
    • They are necessary for glucagon mediated gluconeogenesis during fasting.
    • 📑Hypoglycemic effect of insulin is much stronger (even too strong!) in the absence of glucocorticoids.
    • Both effects above cause hypoglycaemia seen in Addison's disease.
    • The diabetogenic effects are due to increase in protein catabolism, increased gluconeogenesis and decreased peripheral utilization of glucose.
  • Growth hormone: Excess growth hormone worsens diabetes.
    • Mobilizes free fatty acids and favours ketogenesis
    • Decreases glucose uptake into tissues.
    • ⬆ Hepatic glucose output is increased.
  • Exercise: not a hormone BUT
    • 📑Exercise causes insulin independent upregulation of GLUT-4 transporters in muscle increasing glucose uptake.
