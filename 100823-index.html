[!INFO] Definition of Paraneoplastic syndromes
"Paraneoplastic syndromes refer to groupings of symptoms that occur in patients with malignant neoplasms that cannot be readily explained by local invasion or distant metastasis of the tumor, or the elaboration of hormones indigenous to the tissue of origin of the neoplasm. Paraneoplastic syndromes occur in 10–15% of cancer patients."
Source: Sciencedirect preview of Essential Concepts in Molecular Pathology, 2010

Triggered by an abnormal immune response to the tumour, or by substances secreted by the neoplasm itself.

Many cancer patients show CNS involvement.

There are some 'typical praneoplastic syndromes'.

The cancers most commonly associated with paraneoplastic syndromes are

  • small cell lung cancer - PNS are most commonly associated with lung cancer. (PNS is reported in 1 in 10 patients with lung CA)
    • Endocrine paraneoplastic syndromes are the commonest.
  • Breast cancer
  • Gynecologic tumours - ovarian
  • haematologic malignancies.

Endocrine paraneoplastic syndromes

  • Hypercalcemia
    • Associated with poor prognosis
    • One cause is HHM - i.e hypercalcemia associated with Parathyroid hormone related protein (PTHrP) . HHM = humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy (HHM). It is also seen in breast, renal, MM.
    • Squamous cells is the commonest subtype causing this.
    • Another cause is osteolytic lesions.
  • SIADH
    • euvolemic, hypoosmolar hyponatremia.
    • Lung cancer causes ectopic ADH secretion.
    • upto 45% of small cell lung cancer can cause SIADH.
    • Treatment : treat the malignancy itself.
    • Management: similar to normal hyponatremia in SIADH; Free water restriction, Demeclocycline - decreases renal response to ADH, ADH receptor antagonists (cnovaptan, tolvaptan).
  • Ectopic Cushing's syndrome
    • Caused by production of ACTH from tumour cells. ACTH -> increased glucocorticoids -> hypokalemic, metabolic alkalosis.
    • elevated ACTH seen in upto 50% of lung cancer patients.
    • almost always associated with Small Cell Lung Cancer.
    • Confers a worse prognosis.
  • Ectopic growth hormone
    • Can cause acromegaly
    • Secreted from bronchial cardinoids and epidermoid carcinomas.
  • Pulmonary carcinoid syndrome
    • bronchopulmonary neuroendocrine tumours (BP-NET) - not a rare thing. Comprise about 20% of all lung cancers.
    • A small percentage of them develop carcinoid syndrome.
    • Lower incidence of carcinoid syndrome than gut associated carcinoid syndrome.

Neurologic syndromes

Lung cancer is the most common cancer associated with neurologic syndromes.
Mainly associated with Small Cell Lung Cancer (SCLC).
Immune mediated.

  1. Lambert Eaton syndrome
    1. Paraneoplastic LEMS is invariably associated with SCLC.
    2. Autoantibodies bind to Voltage gated calcium channels on the presynaptic membrane and prevent Acetyl-choline release.
    3. Presents as proximal muscle weakness predominanly affecting the hip girdle and lower limbs.
    4. Targeted therapy for symptomatic LEMS with 3,4-diaminopyridine is usually first line.
    5. Refractory cases : immunosuppressants and plasma exchange.
  2. Cerebellar ataxia
  3. Sensory neuropathy
  4. Limbic encephalitis - considered a classic paraneoplastic syndrome.
    1. Inflammation of the limbic system (hippocampus, amygdala, hypothalamus, cingulate gyrus, limbic cortex) causes subacute (days to months) progression of neuropsychiatric symptoms - mood, memory, cognitive function and seizures. EEG and MRI support diagnosis. Can improve with immunotherapy.
    2. Most commonly associated with SCLC. Anti-Hu positive.
  5. encephalomyeltitis

Hypercalcaemia in malignancy

Usually found in advanced disease and is associated with a poor prognosis.

Symptoms: GI symptoms and neuropsychiatric changes.
4 mechanisms of hypercalcemia in malignancy:

  1. Increased PTHrP - humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy-Accounts for 80% of cases of hypercalcemia in malignancy.

    1. Most commonly occurs with squamous cell tumours.
    2. Is the commonest mechanism in lung cancer.

  2. 20% occurs due to osteolytic activity -

    1. Occurs in breast cancer, MM and lymphomas

  3. Tumour secretion of 1-25 dihyroxy vitamin D

  4. Increased parathyroid hormone - a rare mechanism.

  5. ~~or increased G-CSF ~~

Management options:

  1. hydration
  2. Loop diuretics - reduce calcium reabsorption
  3. Bisphosphonates
  4. Calcitonin - in the short term
  5. Denosumab - binds RANKL and prevents osteoclast maturation.

Oncology glossary

Carcinomatosis : rare condition in which cancer spreads creating several tumours in other parts of the body (eg. peritoneal carcinomatosis)

Carcinoid tumours : rare, slow growing neuroendocrine tumours arising from

ECL cells in the GI system and bronchopulmonary systems.

The most common biologically active substance secreted from carcinoid tumors is 5-HT (serotonin), a vasoactive peptide whose biosynthesis is accomplished nearly exclusively by the enterochromaffin cells. Synthesized from the amino acid tryptophan, the release of 5-HT into the systemic circulation can cause the classic symptoms of the carcinoid syndrome, which include diarrhea, episodic flushing, bronchoconstriction, and eventual right-sided valvular heart disease

Lignocaine toxicity

Causes motor and sensory loss when injected near a nerve.
Also antiarrhythmic.
Toxic dose: 4.5mg/Kg
Mechanism: blocks voltage gated sodium channels from the cytoplasmic side and prevents channel activation.

Pharmacokinetics

Highly lipid-soluble and protein-bound local anesthetics have a longer duration of action, presumably because they are less likely to be cleared by blood flow. Local anesthetics that are highly lipid-soluble also exhibit a high degree of plasma protein binding, mostly to alpha-1-acid glycoprotein and, to a lesser extent, albumin; as a direct consequence, their elimination is prolonged.

Symptoms of toxicity

Early symptoms are circumoral numbness, tongue paresthesia, and dizziness. Sensory complaints may include tinnitus and blurred vision. Excitatory signs, such as restlessness, agitation, nervousness, or paranoia, may progress to muscle twitches and seizures.

Cardiac: hypotension, bradycardia, VT, VF, AV blocks

Antidote

Lipid emulsion 20% 1.5ml / Kg and then infusion.

Ivabradin can be used as an antianginal.

Ivabradine is a selective If channel blocker and acts by reducing firing rate of pacemaker cells in the sinoatrial node, without affecting the duration of action potential. The reduction of heart rate causes a reduction of left ventricular end diastolic pressure and increases the time useful to coronary flow by a prolongation of the diastole. A body of evidence found that two drugs are useful in ischemic patients whether at rest or during exercise. In addition, they can be used in monotherapy or in association with other conventional anti-ischemic drugs
