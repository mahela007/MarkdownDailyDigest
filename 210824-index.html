Type 2 Myocardial Infarction

[!INFO] Definition of type 2 MI:
Myocardial infarction: Myocardial damage that occurs due to ischemia.

  • Type 1 MI : MI due to acute atherothrombosis.
  • Type 2 MI : MI due to supply demand mismatch without acute atherothrombosis thrombosis**.
    Source

Both must be differentiated from non-ischemic myocardial injury - i.e not an infarction.

[!INFO] Clinically distinguishing between Type 1 and Type 2 MI
The gold standard to distinguish between the two would theoretically be coronary angiography. However, in reality, it's a clinical judgement.

Presentation of type 2 MI

  • Troponin values are usually lower than in type I MI but there is significant overlap. Source

  • Causes:

    • Hypertension (? increased afterload)
    • arrhythmias (eg tachyarrhythmias increase demand and decrease diastolic myocardial perfusion time)
    • infection
    • severe anemia
    • surgery
    • renal failure
    • and heart failure

  • ECG features

    • ST segment changes cannot reliable differentiate type 1 from type 2.

Simplest way to understand this is through examples:

  • For a patient with suspected DVT, the pretest probability will be calculated by the Well's score.
  • Then a test with a known +ve [[Statistics#Likelihood ratio|likelihood ratio]] is performed. (eg. CTPA or D-dimers)
  • The pretest probability multiplied by the +ve likelihood ration gives the post-test probability

Interpretation of likelihood ratio:

  • positive LR: A positive likelihood ratio (LR+) is the probability that someone with a disease will have a positive test result divided by the probability that someone without a disease will have a positive result.
  • A negative likelihood ratio (LR-) is the probability that someone with a disease will have a negative test result divided by the probability that someone without a disease will have a negative result.
  • The higher the positive likelihood ratio (LR+), the more likely that someone testing positive has the disease in question.
    • The range goes from +1 to approxmately +10. (can be higher) - higher is better.
  • The lower the negative likelihood ratio (LR-), the more likely that someone testing negative does not have the disease in question.
    • The range goes from 1 to 0.1 (lower being better)
