Diabetes insipidus

See [[Electrolyte balance#^e5582a]]

  • Two forms: central ( ⬇ADH secretion) and nephrogenic ( ⬇ADH sensitivity)
  • Diagnosis:
    • First diagnose DI using the water deprivation test: monitor urine osmolality with water deprivation.
    • When DI is confirmed, do DDAVP test to differentiate central from nephrogenic.
    • Plasma copeptin level (Similar principle to C peptide in insulin deficiency). Copeptin the produce of activation of pro-vasopressin.
    • Hypertonic saline infusion test coupled with copeptin level can also be used to differentiate the two types.

Causes of DI:

  • Central: pituitary dysfunction; imaging is indicated; look for other hormonal axis dysfunction
  • Nephrogenic: Majority is aquired. Lithium is a common cause.

As filtrate passes along the CD, water is reabsorbed (enabled and regulated by Vasopressin action) as the CD passes into progressively hypertonic environment of the renal medulla.

[!INFO] Summary

  • Usually present with flu like signs + lymphadenopathy
  • Usually asymptomatic in immunocompetent people. -> No treatment required.
  • Immunosuppresed people get complications -> pyrimethamine is first line drugs.
    • Intracerebral mass lesions are common and chorioretinitis can occur.
  • Latent infection persists lifelong even in immunocompetent people.
  • Vertical transmission only occurs if toxo is acquired during pregnancy.
  • Obligate intracellular parasite.
  • Toxoplasmosis life cycle: definitive host is the cat.
  1. Oocysts are eaten by intermediate host : mice, birds etc.
    2. Consuming undercooked meat (shellfish, cattle etc) containing tissue cysts.
  • Immunocompetent people control the parasite and infection is asymptomatic; encysted parasite remains dormant in host tissues.
  • Immunocompetent people usually don't require treament
  • Immunity is mainly produced by Type 1 cell mediated immunity.
  • However, after initial infection (even if asymptomatic), latent infection will persist for the life of the host.
  • Symptoms are may be flu like; about 15% develop #lymphadenopathy (painless) (usually head and neck) . Symptoms resolve spontaneously in a few weeks to months;
  • Hepatitis, pneumonia and myocarditis, encephalitis are rare complications.

In patients with AIDS, toxoplasmic encephalitis is the most common cause of intracerebral mass lesions and is thought to usually be caused by reactivation of chronic infection.

  • Eye disease: retinochoroiditis -> inflammation and scarring
  • Mother to child transplacental transmission only occurs when infection is acquired during pregnancy as prior infections will be controlled by antibody production.
    • IgM AND IgG Positive -> can be chronic infection but also can be an acute infection because IgG rises quickly.
      • Then check for IgG avidity / affinity.
        Treatment:
  • The goal of treatment is to limit parasite multiplication during active infection.
  • Asymptomatic patients rarely require treatment.
  • For symptomatic patients and especially for eye infection:
  • Pyrimethamine - first line drug; combined with sulfasalazine or clindamycin. TMP-SMX can also be used.

Antiviral drugs

Source
Antiviral drugs do no "kill" viruses; they only inhibit replication.

  • Oseltamivir - selectively inhibits influenza neuraminidase; It inhibits release of viral particles from the host cell.
  • Acylovir - Competitively inhibits viral DNA polymerase of HSV1 and HSV2 and Varicella Zoster virus.
    • Acyclovir is converted to the active form by viral proteins found only within infected cells. Therefore, drug has specificity of action.
  • Anti HIV drugs - bind to the coreceptor and prevent HIV entry into cells.
  • Anti HIV enfuvirtide binds to the HIV protein responsible for fusion of the viral envelope with the cell membrane
  • Reverse transcriptase inhibitors : usually nucleoside or nucleotide analogs; they insert themselves into the replicating DNA and stop the process of reverse transcription. (i.e They work for RNA viruses or DNA viruses with reverse transcription)
    • Zidovudine, Lamivudine work against HIV and Hep B.
  • Integrase inhibitors: Dolutegravira (drugs have 'tegra' in their names)
  • DNA polymerase inhibitors : inhibit the viruses' intrinsically encoded DNA polymerase. Acylovir for herpes virus, ganciclovir for CMV; drugs are usually nucleoside analogs.

Herpes simplex during pregnancy

[!INFO] Summary
New herpes is bad; worst if onset in T3.
Recurrence of herpes doesn't impose a significant risk.

  • Neonatal herpes carries significant mortality risk.
  • Infection of the neonate is by contact with maternal genital secretions.
  • Risk of infection is highest when vaginal delivery occurs within 6 weeks of onset of the ulcer (before maternal antibodies have developed).
  • If the mother develops genital herpes in T2 or earlier, start acylovir from 36 weeks onwards and plan for NVD.
  • If the mother develops new onset genital herpes in T3, start acylovir and plan for LSCS.
  • With recurrent genital herpes, the risk of neonatal infection even with NVD is low. NVD can be offered.

  • Receptive to aldosterone -> increase expression of ENac Channels.
