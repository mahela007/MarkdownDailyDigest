Benign essential tremor

#2020GM-NOV/Q35

  • Everyone has a physiological tremor of 8-12 Hz which is enhanced by anxiety, caffeine, hyperthyroidism and drugs.
  • The amplitude of this is increased in *Benign essential tremor.
  • Features of essential tremor
    • Starts early in life.
    • Bilateral, fast, low amplitude tremors.
    • Presents with bilateral tremmor. Parkinsonian tremmor is usually unilateral at first.
    • The tremor is postural : like when holding a glass.
    • Titubation - thrymic up and down or side to side head movements.
    • Severe impairment of function is rare.
    • There may be a component of intention tremor.
  • It is Familial - #autosomalDominant inheritance
  • Treatment: no effective treatment; alcohol, beta blockers, primidone, or gabapentin may help.
  • Stereotactic thalamotomy and thalamic DBS can be used in severe cases.

Clinical features of hypoparathyroidism Hypocalcemia

Acute hypocalcemia
  • Signs are carpopedtal spasm and tetany.
  • Perioral numbness, paraesthesia of hands and feet

Chronic hypocalcemia
  • In hypoparathyroidism, there is increased bone mineral density (due to decreased turnover) but the bone is not properly formed.
    • So there are increased fractures
  • Features unique to hypocalcemia which occurs due to hypoparathyroidism
    • Extrapyramidal symptoms due to basal ganglia calcification
    • Cataracts
    • Reduced bone turnover
    • Dental abnormalities

laboratory features of hypoparathyroidism

  • Hypocalcemia with low or inapproprirately normal PTH level
  • hyperphosphatemia
  • Normal or low serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25[OH]D)
  • Normal or low 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D - decreased renal activation in the absense of PTH
  • Elevated fractional urinary excretion of Calcium.

Sideroblastic anaemia

A form of anaemia where the defect lies in iron incorporation into haemoglobing. The iron accumulates in mitochondria of erythrocyte precursors which arange around the nucleus. The characteristic histological appearance is of a ring sideroblast. The disease is a laboratory diagnosis.

Antiphospholipid syndrome - APLS / APS

Lupus anticoagulants: "Lupus anticoagulants" actually refers to an anticoagulant effect of several types of antibodies.
However, they actually predispose to THROMBOSIS in vivo.

  • Acquired factor deficiency -> usually due to acquired factor inhibition.
  • Commonest such disease is Acquired factor eight (VIII) deficiency -> "acquired haemophilia A".
  • Predisposing factors : cancer, autoimmune syndrome or postpartum.

Prophylaxis against malaria infection

#2020GM-NOV/Q43

  • Mainly important in travellers.
  • For falciparum (which is chloroquine resistant in most regions) endemic regions:
    • Atovaquone-proguanil - most expensive one but has less side effects.
    • mefloquine - taken weakly; more convenient for long term travellers.
    • Doxycyline - effective against falciparum and other types of malaria. Taken daily and can cause sun sensitization.
    • tafenoquine
    • All high highly effective at preventing malaria.
  • Chloroquine can technically be used if the destination has chloroquine senstive falciparum but this is very rare.
  • For vivax endemic areas, chloroquine and tafenoquine can be given provided the traveller does not have G6PD deficiency.

MCD (minimal change disease)

MCD in adults:
Causes of MCD:

  • Majority are idiopathic.

  • Drugs:

    • NSAID induced
    • Ampicillin
    • Lithium, (mercury - it's also a cause for membranous nephropathy)
    • D-penicillamine
    • Malignancies, particulartly haematologic ones: Hodgkin lymphoma and non hodgkin lymphoma (paraneoplastic)

  • Infections like syphilis, TB, mycoplasma.

  • The subepithelial deposits cause the basement membrane to thicken with visible 'spike' formation. Spikes eventually encase the deposited immune complexes.

  • Most often idiopathic.

    • Need to exclude hepatitis B, SLE, thyroiditis, some drugs (penicillamine, gold / mercury, captopril)

    • [!INFO] Mnemonic: hepatitis B - memBranous

Minimal change Vs. FSGS

MCD and FSGS are like cousins; FSGS is the evil cousin.
📑Haematuria is commoner in FSGS than in MCD.
📑MCD shows selective protein loss -> i.e mainly albumin and not higher molecular weight proteins like immunoglobulins. FSGS shows non-selective proteinuria.
📑FSGS shows poor response to corticosteroids.
FSGS : FSGS changes are visible on light microscopy. MCD: few changes visible on light microscopy.

Management of IgA nephropathy

#2020SBR-NOV/Q6

  • Mainstay is optimized supportive care with

    • Blood pressure control
    • RAAS inhibitors for control of proteinuria;
    • Immunosuppessive therapy is a last resort because iffective immunosuppressive therapy, while effective has significant side effects.

  • All patients with or without hypertension should receive either ACEi or ARB to reduce proteinuria and preserve renal function. If persistently proteinuric on ACEi or ARB, change to the other drug or add SGLT-2 inhibitor.

    • If proteinuria > 1g / day persists persists for 3 months with supportive measures above, add a steroid. (as these patients have a higher risk of progressing to ESKD).

  • Tonsillectomy can reduce proteinuria and haematuria in patients with recurrent tonsilitis.

Hereditary Haemochromatosis (HH)

[!INFO] Classic triad of features: Liver, pancreas and skin.
Ferritin levels > 300 in males or 200 in females suggest iron overload and ferritin > 1000 strongly suggests the need for phlebotomy.

  • People with organ injury from iron overload : Phlebotomy
  • People with isolated elevations in ferritin or transferrin saturation :
    • Ferritin can be elevated due to alcoholic liver disease itself. Cessation of alcohol - and treating other causes of liver dysfunction - is very important in management.
    • Phlebotomy and expectant management are both options for people with isolated elevation of ferritin or TSAT.
  • Heterozygous individuals
    • Iron overload is very rare in them.
