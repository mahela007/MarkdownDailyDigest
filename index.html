Pemphigus vulgaris

  • Rare, life threatening
  • Antibodies against desmosomal cadherin found between epidermal cells. -> intraepidermal blister in skin and mucous membranes -> erosions
  • Oral lesions are common (70%) - can precede skin lesions
  • Pemphigus: Painful, Intraepidermal, flaccid, fragile blisters and itchy erosions.
  • Nikolsky sign +ve <- rubbing normal skin causes sloughing.
  • Usually occurs in adulthood.
  • Recognised triggers: ACEi, rifampicin and penicillamine.
  • Can have paraneoplastic association with non-hodgkin's lymphoma or chronic lymphocytic leukemia.

[!INFO] Nikolsky sign
Simple version: top layers of the skin separate from the lower layers.
Complex version:
Lateral pressure applied to the border of a blister causes displacement of the epidermis and extension of the blister.
There is weakening of the assoociation between corneal and granular layers because of breakdown of desmosomes.
Apparently normal skin between the obvious blistering lesions is also affected.
Nikolsky sign is pathognomonic for

  • pemphigus,
  • toxic epidermal necrolysis, and
  • staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome
  • Rarer than pemphigus vulgaris. No oral lesions.
  • Subcorneal blistering is seen.
  • manifests in UV irratidated skin sites.

Geriatric medicine

[!INFO] The main questions in geriatric medicine - Kumar and Clark
In Geriatric medicine, a balance has to be found between

  1. What can be done for the patient and
  2. What should be done for the patient.
    • Edmunt Pellegrino

Common problems in geriatric medicine are

  • Frailty - #2021GM-JUL Q02
    decreased physiological reserve of the organ systems. In a frail person, a given physiological stress will have a greater negative impact on the organ systems.

    • Frailty does not necessarily correspond with age; it is possible to be young and frail and elderly and not frail. eg: fit person -> uti -> minimal symptoms.
    • frail person -> uti -> delerium etc.
    • Therefore, a patient must be managed according to their functional status and *not their chronological age.
    • 3 or more of the criteria define the presence of frailty.

  • Sarcopenia: progressive, systemic muscle disorder which leads to loss of muscle fibers, in particular fast twitch fibers.

    • Increased risk of falls and fractures
    • Maximum amount of oxygen consumption (VO2 max) reduces.
    • Gait speed reduces
    • Reduced energy expenditure ensues.
    • This somehow causes an energy deficit (probably due to anorexia of aging)-> not enough ATP for apoptosis -> cells instead undergo necrosis -> Even more ATP is used up -> cycle propagates -> more sarcopenia -> frailty cycle

  • Multimorbidity - presence of two or more medical conditions that may or may not interact.

    • Multimorbidity can lead to prescribing cascades and co prescription (omeprazole added to aspirin therapy)

  • Disability : Physical or mental impairment which has substantial and long term negative impact on the ability of a person to carry out daily activities.
