[!INFO] How stem cell transplants work
"Almost right away after the stem cells are infused into your body, they’ll travel through your bloodstream to your bone marrow. They’ll start to divide and create healthy new blood cells. Engraftment is when your body accepts the transplant and your blood cell counts start to recover." Source

Engraftment usually takes about 10 to 14 days. It can take longer, depending on the source of your stem cells. Platelets and red blood cells usually take a little longer to recover than white blood cells.

Can occur after allogenic haemotopoietic cell transplantation or with solid organs containing lymphoid tissue. (Bowel transplant was one example)

  • Acute GVHD (aGVHD) – Characterized by a rapid onset and acute disease course. Occurs within 100 days of the transplant.
    • Symptoms consist of a triad of
      • Dermatitis - painful, pruritic rash.
      • Hepatitis - can progress to liver failure
      • Enteritis - diarrhoea, intestinal bleeding and ileus
  • Chronic GVHD (cGVHD) – Characterized by a chronic disease course that can involve virtually all organs with variable manifestations, including sclerosis;
    • Milder involvement of all organ systems
    • Things like chronic cough, asthma like symptoms, dry mucosa, arthralgia and arthirits, muscle cramps, mouth dryness, dysphagia,occular irritation etc.

Chronic granulomatous disease

#2022BSQ-MAY Q57
Is a cause for staphylococcal infections.
The defects are either #x-linked-recessive recessive or #autosomal-Recessive.
The defects prevent WBCs from producing free radicals which are required to kill phagocytosed bacteria.
I.e Phagocytosis is intact but killing is impaired.
Presentation: Recurring infections (due to catalase producing organisms like staph aureus, E. coli, klebsiella etc) since childhood or early adolscence with systemic granuloma formation (lungs, liver, lymph nodes etc).
Treatment: Prophylactic antibiotics (TMP-SMX) and in severe infection, granulocyte transfusion. Interferron gamma is also used for treatment.

TNF-alpha

TNF family receptors are 'death receptors' -> they trigger apoptosis within the cell.
Apoptosis of cells is mediated by caspases. They are enzymes which degrade DNA, nuclear matrix and cytoskeleton.
There are two pathways of apoptosis -

  1. mitochondrial or intrinsic pathway => promoted by DNA damange and accumulation of misfolded proteins. Pro apoptotic proteins leak out of the mitrochondria; This pathway is inhibited when the cell receives growth signals.
  2. Death receptor (extrinsic) pathways => Involved in apoptosis of self reactive lymphocytes and in cell death triggered by cytotoxic T lymphocytes.

Endothelial cell contraction occurs rapidly after binding of histamine, bradykinin, leukotrienes, and many other mediators to specific receptors, and is usually short-lived (15 to 30 minutes). A slower and more prolonged retraction of endothelial cells, resulting from changes in the cytoskeleton, may be induced by cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor (TNF) and interleukin-1 (IL-1).

TNF is pro inflammatory: It does things like promote endothelial retraction (for increased vessel permeability) and increased promote leukocyte chemotaxis.

Leukocyte rolling is mediated by selectins and leukocyte attachment to endothelium is mediated by integrins (on the neutrophils). TNF and other cytokines promote expression of selectins and integrin ligands on endothelial cells.
TNF is produced by tissue macrophages that are responding to a pathogen.

In acute inflammation, neutrophils predominate from 6-24 hours. Then monocytes arrive from 24-48 hours.

The major cytokines mediating acute inflammation are

  1. TNF
  2. IL-1
  3. IL-6
  4. chemokines - chemoattractant cytokines.

TNF and IL-1 : the main role of TNF and IL-1 is in endothelial cell activation which causes increased vascular permeability and increased expression of adhesion molecules to facillitate phagocyte (neutrohil) migration and to enhance production of chemokines and other cytokines.

TNF also increases the thrombogenicity of endothelium. IL-1 activates tissue fibroblasts, resulting in increased proliferation and production of ECM.

TNF and IL-1, in addition to local effects, have systemic effects; they trigger the acute phase response.

Cytokines more important in chronic inflammation

  1. Interferon gamma
  2. IL-12

Antirheumatoid drugs

First line treatment :

  1. DMARDS (especially methotrexate). - Alters disease progression.
  2. NSAIDS - symptomatic relief only.

DMARDS

Methotrexate, sulfasalazine, gold compounds, penicillamine, chloroquine and other antimalarials.

How many DMARDS work is bascially still a mystery. But they do reduce symptoms and reduce disease activity.
DMARDs takes months to act.

Methotrexate

Cytotoxic and immunosuppresant.
More rapid onset of action than other DMARDs.
Good efficacy. Generally well tolerated.
Beware of fatal blood dyscrasias and cirrhocis!!
It is also an antifolate drug but this is not it's mechanism of anti rheumatoid action. (Therfore, folate is co administered). Antirheumatic action may be due to blockage of adnosine uptake.

Sulfasalazine

Complex of sulphonamide and salicylate.
Mechanism not exactly known.
Usually well tolerated but risk of reversible skin reaction and leukopenia.
Poorly absorbed orally.
Impairs folic acid absorption -> given with folic acid.

Penicillamine

Produced by hydrolysis of penicillin.
Has metal chelating properties which are used in Wilson's disease. (Should NOT be GIVEN with gold compounds)
Mechanism of action not really known.
Probably reduced immune response and IL-1 generation.
Side effects are common - Rashes, stomatitis; altered taste due to zinc chelation.
Causes Proteinuria in 20%.(membranous glomerulonephritis)
Absolute contraindications: Autoimmune diseases, leukopenia, aplastic anaemia.

Gold compounds

Sodium aurothiomalate - an organic gold complex.
Action takes 3 - 4 months.
Mechanism unknown but disease activity is reduced.
Gold complexes accumulate in many tissues including synovial tissue.
Given by deep IM injection -> intially every 7 days but as the half life increases, later on given monthly.
Rate of severe side effects is quite high (1/10) -> mouth ulcers, severe skin rashes, proteinuria,

Antimalarials

Chloroquine - used when other treatments have failed.
Takes about a month to act.
Contraindicated in psoriatic arthropathy because it worsens skin lesions.

Immunosuppressant drugs

Most act during the induction phase of the immune response -> they reduce lymphocyte proliferation.

Mechanisms Drugs
Inhibition of IL-2 ciclosporin, tacrolimus
Inhibit cytokine gene expression corticosteroids
Inhibition of purine and pyrimidine synthesis azthioprine, MMF

Calcineurin inhibitors

Cyclosporin

#2022BSQ-MAY Q33
Discovered in 1972, made by fungi.
Calcineurin inhibitor. Calcineurin is inolved in activation of transcription factors responsible for IL-2 production when a T cell is stimulated by and antigen.
cyclosporin gets concentrated in tissues rather than plasma.
Discovery enabled transplant surgery.
Immunosupressive activity without cytotoxicity!.
Inihibits action of IL-2 leading to decreased prolifration of T cells.
Nephrotoxicity is a common side effect.
Hepatotoxicity can occur.
Lethargy, hirsutism, paraesthesis.
Need serum level monitoring.

Tacrolimus

Very similar action to cyclosporin
but much more potent!
Higher incidence of nephrotoxicity and neurotoxicity.
Less hirsutism.
Hyperglycemia can occur.
Used mainly in organ transplantation and in severe atopic eczema.

Purine inhibitors

Azathioprine

Cytotoxic.
Inhibits purine synthesis.
Used for autoimmune diseases and transplant medicine.
Bone marrow suppression can occur.

Cyclophosphamide

Potent immunosuppresant.
last line drug in rheumatoid arthritis because of significant toxic effects.
Is an akylating agent: forms covalent bonds with DNA and prevents cell replication -> used as an anticancer drug.
Important toxic effects are nausea and vomiting, bone marrow depression and haemorrhagic cystitis. Haemorrhagic cystitis can be prevented by good hydration and NAC (binds with toxic metabolite of cyclphosphamide called acrolein)

Mycophenolate mofetil

Another derivative of a fungal antibiotic.
Prevents de novo purine synthesis specifically in T and B cells.
Used to prevent organ rejection.
Given orally. Aluminium and Mg hydroxide will impair absorption.

Leflunomide

used mainly for rheumatoid arthritis.
Specifically inhibits activated T cells by inhibiting pyrimidine synthesis.
Diarrhoea, alopecia.
Long half life
Given orally
