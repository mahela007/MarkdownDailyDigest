Risk factors for pregnancy associated hypertensive disorders

Conditions that reduce uteroplacental blood flow

  1. "metabolic syndrome like factors"
    1. pre-existing hypertension
    2. DM
    3. OSA
    4. BMI > 30
  2. Previous personal history
    1. Previous history of pre-eclampsia or HELLP
  3. possible genetic link
    1. first degree relative with gestational hypetension
  4. Pregnancy associated factors
    1. First time mother
    2. Twin or higher gestation
  5. Thrombophilia
  6. Autoimmune disease

Pathogensis of pregnancy associated hypertensive disorders

[!TIP] Systemic endthelial dysfunction is key!

Current research demonstrates that improper trophoblast differentiation during endothelial invasion due to abnormal regulation and/or production of cytokines, adhesion molecules, major histocompatibility complex molecules, and metalloproteinases plays a key role in the development of a gestational hypertensive disease.  Abnormal regulation and/or production of these molecules leads to abnormal development and remodeling of spiral arteries in the deep myometrial tissues. This leads to placental hypoperfusion and ischemia. More recent research shows role of antiangiogenic factors that are released by placental tissue cause systemic endothelial dysfunction which can result in systemic hypertension. (in PIH, activity of angiogenic factors like VEGF and PlGF - placental growth factor - are reduced) Organ hypoperfusion from endothelial dysfunction is most commonly seen in the eyes, lungs, liver, kidneys and peripheral vasculature. Overall, most experts agree underlying reason is multifactorial

Pharmacotherapy for hypertension in pregnancy

Drugs to avoid in pregnancy

Avoid propranolol and other non selective beta blocker -> can increase uterine irritability. Atenolol has been associated with lower foetal weight. Avoid.
Avoid nitroprusside -> risk of foetal cyanide poisoning.

Hydralazine

Oral Hydralazine monotheraphy maintenance therapy should be avoided as it can cause reflex tachycardia.

Treatment cutoffs

Severe hypertension:
urgent treatment:
if SBP > 160 Or DBP > 110 - confirmed by a second measurement within 15 minutes - should be treated within 60 minutes with labetalol or hydralzine to reduce risk of stroke, heart failure and other complications.

Mean arterial pressure should not be reduced by more than 25% over two hours, systolic blood pressure should not be reduced below 130 mmHg, and diastolic blood pressure should not be reduced below 80 mmHg. Blood pressures in the range of 130 to 150/80 to 100 mmHg are ideal. - UpToDate

Non severe hypertension:
Treat with oral medications :
SBP>140 OR DBP > 90;
oral medications are the same 4 as those listed above.
BP targets are generally SBP < 140 and DBP < 90;

Hypertension in the post partum period

Normal physiology : blood pressure decreases immediately after delivery but then increases in a few days and remains elevated above baseline levels.

Postpartum hypertension may be due to persistence of antepartum or intrapartum hypertension or may be of new onset.
"post partum preeclampsia" refers to the identification of features of preeclampsia only following delivery. - this happens in around 5% of patients.

Options for pharmacotherapy in post partum period: (safety in breast feeding)

  1. CCBs are safe.
  2. ACE inhibitors - transferred in breatmilk in low concentrations. may be risky
  3. Beta blockers : propranolol, metoprolol and labetalol are safe.
    1. Atenolol is transferred in milk and can affect the baby.
  4. HCQ : may reduce milk volume, but safe in low doses.
  5. Hydralazine and methyl dopa - safe.
