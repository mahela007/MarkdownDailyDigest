Autoimmune response to nephritogenic GABHS (strep. pyogenes) -> Immune complexes (IgG and complement) are formed and deposited in glomeruli -> activation of alternate complement pathway (hypocomplimentaemia occurs) -> recruitment of inflammatory cells -> glomerular injury.
Also called mesangiocapilllary or mesangioproliferative glomerulonephritis.

Polygenic inheritance pattern -

  • genes but possible env factor interact to produce Parkinson's disease.
  • Majority of cases are sporadic.
  • but #autosomalDominant and #autosomal-Recessive forms of the disease also exist, although rare.
  • Autosomal dominant PD is caused by mutations of the alpha synuclein gene.

Pathogenesis: Loss of dopaminergic neurons in pars compacta of substantia nigra of midbrain which project to the striatum (= caudate nucleus + putamen) of the basal ganglia. 70% loss has occured when symptoms appear.

[!INFO] The nigrostriatal pathway

One of the 4 major domapinergic pathways in the brain. Source

Pathology:

  • Lewy bodies (neuronal inclusions made of alpha-synuclein and ubiquitin) seen first in medulla -> pons -> substantia nigra.
  • palor of the substantia nigra
  • Lewy neurites (dystrophic neurites contained the inclusions)

Chronic pancreatitis

#2021BSQ-JUL/Q07

Multiple causes:
Alcohol is a common cause. CKD, Cystic fibrosis, Obstruction, trauma, recurrent acute pancreatitis, hypercalcemia, autoimmune are all causes. Source

There is loss of endocrine and exocrine function but significant failure occurs only after many years.

  • Exocrine failure is not clinically apparent until 90% of the pancreas is destroyed.

  • Occurs either because trypsin is activated before it leaves or is unable to leave once activated.

  • Ethanol does this by increasing intracellular calcium which causes trypsin activation but alcohol must be only one factor of many which act together.

  • Genetic factors (SPINK, PRSS1) which affect trypisin activation have been implicated in the pathogenesis.

  • CFTR gene (Cyctic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator) function is required for production of high volume bicarbonate rich pancreatic juice which flushes out trypsin. Therefore, Cystic fibrosis is associated with chronic pancreatitis.

    • In cystic fibrosis patients, pancreatic exocrine insuffiency will develop in utero or the perinatal period. They will develop malabsorption and failure to thrive.

  • Autoimmune pancreatitis Type I (the commonest type) is seen in men. IgG4 positive with dense lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate.

    • Autoimmune pancreatitis responds well to steroids and lack of response should arouse suspicion of another diagnosis. Azathioprine is used for relapses.

[!INFO] Mnemonic: Men drink -> pancreatitis common -> autoimmune pancreatitis is also commoner in men

Investigations in chronic pancreatis

  • Elevated serum amylase (in advanced disease, may be normal due to exocrine insufficiency)
  • Serum IgG4 levels -> autoimmune panceatitis
  • Low faecal elastase
  • Abdominal USS
  • SPINK, PRSS1 and CFTR gene mutations
  • CECT
  • MRI and MRCP
  • Diagnostic ERCP has been replaced by MRCP

Management

Smoking is a risk factors -> smoking cessation is recommended in management.

  • Pain: NSAIDS, tramadol, coeliac nerve block, TCA antidepressants for chronic pain, pregabalin.
  • Steatorrhoea: usually occurs with long standing chronic pancreatitis. Enzyme supplements are given.
    • note that inadequate pancreatic bicarbonate secretion also contributes by ?inhibiting enzyme activation in the duodenum
    • Fat intake should NOT be reduced - will only worsen malnutrition.

Complications

Pancreatic pseudocyst, ascites, pleural effusion, pancreatic cancer.

Pancreatic diabetes

AKA type 3c diabeties or pancreatogenic diabetes.
Occurs after many years of disease progression.
A type of 'brittle' diabetes : patients have insulin deficiency (but rare ketosis) and also glucagon deficiency so they develop hypoglycaemia very easily while on treatment.
