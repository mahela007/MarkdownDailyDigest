Genital Chlamydia infection

  • Chlamydia is extremely common (1/10 young women in the UK)
  • Most carriers are asymptomatic (which is why it's so widespread). Oppurtunistic testing is the mainstay of disease control.
  • Incubation is 1 to 2 weeks.
  • Testing should be done 2 weeks after exposure.
  • red staining inclusion bodies are seen in infected cells.
  • Complications: epididymitis, perihepatitis, ⬆ecopic risk, endometritis, infertility.
  • Diagnosis: NAAT is used.
  • Management Doxycycline; azithro is no longer preferred due to high rate of coninfection with mycoplasma genitalium (which is resistant to azithro)
    • If pregnant then azithromycin
  • Contact screening is done.

Addison's disease

  • Adrenal insufficiency can be primary, secondary or tertiary.
  • Secondary and tertiary are collectively called "central" adrenal insufficiency.
  • Primary adrenal insufficience is also called Addison's disease
  • Caused by destruction of the cortex of the adrenal glands.
  • Symptoms appear when 90% of the cortex is destroyed.
  • Tuberculosis is the most common cause of Addison's disease worlwide;
    • Other causes: autoimmune, meningitis + adrenal haemorrhage, amyloidosis, infections (such as AIDS linked conditions)
    • Autoimmune: associated with vitiligo, type 1 DM etc.
  • Viral infection spread by the dengue mosquito.
  • occurs in South America and Sub-saharan africa.
  • Patient presents with fever and severe flu-like illness.
  • Most infections are mild.
  • In about 1/5th of patients, the illness initially resolves and then recurts in a so called period of intoxication phase.
  • (usual progression is infection, remission, intoxication)
  • In the intoxication phase, patient develops
    • AKI, hepatitis with jaundice and bleeding
  • It is a WHO notifiable disease.
  • Management is supportive.

Monogenic diabetes mellitus

  • Previously called MODY. (maturity onset diabetes of the young)
  • Caused by a single gene mutation which is dominantly inherited. #autosomalDominant (Mnemonic: MODY --> autosomal DOMYant)
  • This affects beta cell function.
  • Suspect in a patient with early onset diabetes with a family history (almost 50% of relatives or a parent) of the same.
  • Classic type 1 DM does not develop in infants younger than 6 months of age. Monogenic diabetes is likely if DM occurs at this age. -> ie. the glucokinase mutation variant.
    Pathogenesis:
  • Diabetes results from a defect in insulin RELEASE an therefore, responds well to sulfonylureas.
  • The commonest subtype if HNF-1a. They have low renal glucose threshold with glycosuria and elevated HDL. Defect is on Chromosome 12q.
  • There are other HNF subtypes, each having a few unique features. See Kumar and Clark box 23.6.
  • Management depends on the subtype
    • Glucokinase - aka MODY 2 - no management necessary.
    • HNF types -> respond well to sulfonylureas.
  1. In humans, tissues cysts are most commonly formed in skeletal muscle, myocardium, brain and eyes.
  • Symptoms are may be flu like; about 15% develop #lymphadenopathy (painless) (usually head and neck) . Symptoms resolve spontaneously in a few weeks to months;
    • Parasites can be visualized with Giemsa stain after making impression smears of lesions.

Management of ACNE

  • Acne is caused by hyperproliferation of keratinocytes in the pilocebaceous follicle resulting in obstruction.
  • Within this environment, various changes result in formation of acne.
  • Propioni bacterium acne is implicated in the pathogensis.
  • Synthesis of testosterone in the follicle causes increased production of sebum.
    Therapeutic options:
  1. Comedolytic topics medications - benzoyl peroxide, salicylic acid, and retinoids.
  2. Systemic: isotretinoin and antiandgoren treatment.
    1. Antiandrogens are contraindicated in men.
    2. Isotretinoin reduces sebum production; Vitamin A derivative, contraindicated in pregnancy (even blood donation is contraindicated while on it); congenital malformations can result.
    3. Usually very effective for acne.
  3. Antibiotics supposedly kill the bacteria:
    1. Topical clindamycin
    2. Oral doxycycline
