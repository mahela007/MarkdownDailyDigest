Commonest cause of Cushing's syndrome is iatrogenic;

  • All glucocorticoids suppress ACTH -> ACTH, and serum cortisol will be low in iatrogenic cushing's syndrome.

The diagnosis of Cushing syndrome is established when at least two different first-line tests are unequivocally abnormal, and physiologic hypercortisolism has been excluded
[!INFO] TLDR;
3 confirmatory tests for confirm hypercortisolism

  1. BTSC
  2. 24-UFC
  3. LD-DST

3 localization tests once hypercortisolism is confirmed

  1. HD-DST
  2. CRH-ST
  3. Desmopressin-ST
  • One of these is done; if positive, it confirms pituitary source of ACTH. If negative, it confirms ectopic ACTH.
  • If cortisol is found to be elevated with the above preliminary tests, next step is to exclude physiologic hypercortisolism.
  • AKA - pseudo Cushing syndrome.
  • Clinically, these patients usually do not have skin or muscle manifestations of Cushing Syndrome.
  1. Severe major depression
    1. insulin tolerance test can be used to differentiate this.
    2. Insulin induced hypoglycaemia will cause a normal rise in cortisol in a depressed patient but no rise in true cushing's syndrome.
  2. Poorly controlled DM
  3. OSA
  4. Malnutrition

[!INFO] These tests are called "Localisation tests".

  • First line test for this is measuring ACTH levels.
  • Do 9am and midnight plasma ACTH.
  • Because of cyclical variations, testing at two different times is recommended.
  • ACTH < 5pg/ML = ACTH independent hypercortisolism
  • ACTH > 20pg/mL = ACTH dependent hypercortisolism.
    1. Corticotroph pituitary tumours respond to CRH (despite being tumours).
      1. A rise in cortisol with CRH stimulation confirms a pituitary source of ACTH.
  1. High dose dexamethasone suppression test
    1. Corticotroph tumours retain sensitivity to negative FB by glucocorticoids
  2. Desmopressin stimulation test
    1. Used clinically when CRH is not available
    2. ADH stimulates ACTH release.
    3. Desmopressin is used to mimic this effect. (ADH and desmopressin have a similar action to CRH)

Ectopic adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) syndrome accounts for 10%–20% of the cases of ACTH-dependent Cushing's syndrome (1). Ectopic ACTH secretion typically results from an occult, slow-growing bronchial carcinoid tumor. Source
