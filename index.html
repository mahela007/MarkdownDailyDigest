Definition:
Absolute neutrophil count > 7700

Neutrophils are produced in marrow. 50% are not actively circulating because if margination.
Therefore, neutrophilia can be caused by

  • increased production
  • decreased margination

Observation without immediate further testing is reasonable for clinically stable patients with modest neutrophilia (eg, <20,000/microL) and no worrisome clinical or laboratory findings from the initial evaluation

Causes of neutrophilia

[!INFO] SUMMARY
infection
Malignancy
Inflammatory (automimmune)
Iatrogenic

Common causes

  • Infection
  • iatrogenic:
    • glucocorticoids
    • G-CSF
  • cigarette smoking - commonest cause but only mild neutrophilia
  • myeloproliferative disorders : CML, PV, ET
    • (CML) typically presents with the entire spectrum of maturing granulocytic cells (eg, bands, metamyelocytes, myelocytes, promyelocytes, blasts) in the peripheral blood, along with thrombocytosis, polycythemia, eosinophilia and/or basophilia, and splenomegaly

Less common causes

  • physical or emotional stress, vigorous exercise, heatstroke
    • Neutrophilia can occur within minutes of exercise, stress, or epinephrine injection
  • autoimmune inflammation - (JIA, IBD, Kawasaki, RA etc.)
    • there's no direct way to establish inflammation as the cause for neutrophilia other than by excluding infection and malignancy. The blood picture won't show anything special
  • asplenia / hyposplenism

Spurious

  • Platelet clumping
  • cryoglobulinemia

Association with increases or decreases in other cells lines can be helpful in narrowing down the possible cause.

Findings which should raise concern

Clinical:

  • Ill patient ? infection
  • splenomegaly -? Malignancy
  • weight loss, fevers, sweats - ?malignancy

Lab:

  • left shift - infection but also chronic myeloid leukemias
  • toxic granulation - suspect infection
  • blasts

Moderate neutrophilia (<20,000) in a well patient can be monitored without urgent intervention.

  • Causes of leukocytosis
  • Evaluation of abdominal pain
  • Presentation of typhoid
  • LRs for various hypo and hyperthyroid feature's.

[!NOTE] ECG lesson 1:

  • Be vigilant for evidence of posterior MI (ST dips in V 1,2,3 with tall R waves) in any patient with an inferior or lateral STEMI.
  • takotsubo presents just like a STEMI. Only angiogram will differentiate the two.
  • As there is no formal definition/criteria for hyperacute T waves (HATW). , the area under the curve is more important than the height in HATW, which often present initially as wide, bulky T waves.
  • Always assess a patient's general appearance to detect the possibility of endocrine disease.
