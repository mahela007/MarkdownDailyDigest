[!INFO] Timelines for STEMI management
Source <- AHA guidelines; see Hypothesis highlights.

  • PCI is a valid option if the patient present within 12 hours of symptom ONSET.
    • If time since onset is > 3 hours, primary PCI is generally preferred.
  • Once the patient does present to healthcare services:
  • Target door to balloon time for STEMI PCI 90 minutes. (lowered from previous recommendation of 120 minutes).
  • If the patient is to receive fibronolysis rather than PCI (for whatever reason):
  • Target door to needle time should be < 30 minutes.
  • Transfer to a PCI capable center (instead of primary PCI):
    • Is an acceptable alternative if door to balloon time will still be < 90 minutes since presentation to the transferring hospital.
    • In such a case, if the door to balloon time exceeds the door to needle time by >= 60 minutes, fibrinolysis is preferred.
  • Ideally, the '90 minutes' is measured since first contact with healcare services. If this is EMS, then EMS to balloon time should be < 90 minutes. If first presentation is to hospital, then door to balloon time should be < 90 minutes.

Selecting a reperfusion strategy

The higher the risk of bleeding for the partient, the more PCI is preferred over thrombolysis.

Cardiac glycoside - Found in [[Plant toxins|Kaneru]]
[[VaughanWilliamsClassificationAntiarrhythmics.png]]

Cardiac glycosides reversibly inhibit the sodium-potassium-ATPase, causing an increase in intracellular sodium and a decrease in intracellular potassium.

  1. Heart failure - inhibition of Na/K ATPase -> increased intra-myocyte sodium -> decreased Na calcium exchange -> reduced secretion of calcium -> increased intracellular calcium -> contractility increased.
    In patients with HF, digoxin also inhibits sympathetic outflow and augments parasympathetic tone.
  • Increased parasympathetic tone to the SA node and AV node -> (via 📑 hypersensitization of carotid sinus baroreceptors, central stimulation, increased vagal output, and possible potentiation of the effect of acetylcholine on the sinoatrial [SA] node) -> 📑slowing the firing of the SA node and prolonging conduction at the atrioventricular (AV) node.
    Antiarrhythmic - slows conduction through the AV node, increases vagal tone. This property is used to slow the ventricular rate in patients with persistent AF.

📑Avoid in acute coronary syndrome

These effects occur at therapeutic doses

  • Downsloping ST depression - 'salvador dali' or 'reverse tick' appearance.
  • Biphasic T waves.(negative deflection followed by positive) (or even flattened of inverted T waves)
  • The preceding two changes are used by decreased in the atrial and ventricular refractory periods and alterations in ventricular repolorization.
  • Shortening of QT interval
  • Mild prolongation of the PR interval due to increased vagal tone to the AV node.

Digoxin toxicity

Factors predisposing to toxicity

  • Has a narrow therapeutic window
  • Especially hypokalemia or hypomagnesemia can potentiate toxicity.
  • Hypercalcemia can increase risk of toxicity. Hypocalcemia can nullify digoxin effects. -> maintain normal electrolyte levels.
  • Renal dysfunction increases digoxin levels and often precipitate toxicity!
  • Drug interactions
    • Amiodarone and verapamil displace digoxin from tissue binding sites and reduce renal excretion

Acute toxicity

Acute toxicity is uncommon: Effects are

  • Hyperkalemia (inhibition of Na/K ATPase),
  • confusion and weakness is COMMON
  • hypomagnesemia,
  • hypoxia will predispose to exagerrated action of digoxin. - Correct before administration.

Chronic toxicity

  • Hypokalemia
  • Symptoms - GI: N/V/D Dizziness, CNS may be prominent : Confusion, Delirium
  • Bradycardia
  • Supraventricular tachycardias (?due to increase automaticity) with or without heart block
  • Ventriular tachycardia
  • Ventricular ectopics (premature ventricular complexes)
  • Hyperkalemia (inhibition of Na/K Pump)
  • 📑Side effect: Gynaecomastia

[!WARNING] Common effects of digoxin toxicity on the ECG
Other common dysrhythmias associated with digoxin toxicity include:

  • Frequent PVCs (the most common abnormality), including ventricular bigeminy and trigeminy
  • Sinus bradycardia
  • Slow Atrial Fibrillation
  • Any type of AV block (1st degree, 2nd degree & 3rd degree)
  • Regularised AF = AF with complete heart block and a junctional or ventricular escape rhythm
  • Ventricular tachycardia, including polymorphic and bidirectional VT
