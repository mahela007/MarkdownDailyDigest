[!TIP] The two organisms causing the most severe forms of disease infect bone marrow.

  • Newer articles state that vivax also infects bone marrow. Source
    Vertical tranmission:
  • All species of malaria can be transmitted in utero.
  • Most commonly with Falciparum and vivax.
  • Falciparum is well known to cause transplacental transmission. The parasite can be found in intervillous spaces of the placenta.(placental infection) This causes placental inflammation and various placental abnormalities resulting in IUGR, premature delivery, low birth weight.Source
    Falciparum infection of RBC -> RBC produces sticky knobs of proteins -> RBC adhere to capillary endothelim -> sequestration -> partial blood flow obstruction -> compliactions like cerebral malaria and renal failure.

[!INFO] Summary

  • Uncomplicated non falciparum malaria
    • Treatment is directed at the erythrocytic forms.
    • If species unknown of patient develops severe malaria -> treat as for falciparum.
    • If chloroquine sensitive, give chloroquine or ACT.
    • If chloroquine resistant, give ACT.
    • Fever and parasitaemia stellet in 2 - 4 days.
    • Antirelapse therapy with primaquine is started when symptoms begin to resolve.
  • Treatment of severe malaria:
    • If no ACT resistance, give IV artesunate.
    • If ACT resistance is prevalent (south east asia) -> combine IV quinine with IV ACT.

Treatment of malaria in #pregnancy

ACT is safe in pregnancy
Chloloquine is safe in pregnancy.
Primaquine is problematic because it exacerbates G6PD-deficiency anaemia;

  • Therefore, it's contraindicated in pregnancy as the G6PD status of the foetus is unknown.
  • Although breast milk concentrations are low, it's not given while the mother is breastfeeding if the infant is found to be G6PD deficient.
  • In the above cases, chloroquine is continued until the baby is delivered or until cessation of breast feeding.

[[Nephrology miscellaneous#Facts about renal blood flow]]

Renal blood flow

Facts about renal blood flow:

Source

  • Renal arteries are end arteries. [[whatIsAnEndArtery.png]]

  • There is a high pressure capillary bed (glomerular) and low pressure capillary bed (peritubular).

  • 95% of renal blood flow goes to the cortex! only 5% to the medulla. Renal blood flow is high not because of metabolic demand but because it's needed for filtration.

  • Blood flow autoregulation works in the MAP range of 75 to 160.

    • Autoregulation is maintained by
      • Myogenic response - 50%
      • Tubuloglomerular feedback (35%)
      • Mechanisms involving angiotensin II and NO (<15%)

  • Sympathetic stimulation reduces the range under which auto-regulation is possible. The lowest pressure at which autoregulation is possible is increased by sympathetic stimulation; i.e In hypotension, sympathetic stimulation acts to reduce renal blood flow.

  • Factors decreasing renal blood flow

    • Noradrenaline - decreases renal blood flow.
    • Adrenaline -> increased resistance and decreased renal blood flow.
    • Angiotensin II- Efferent > afferent constriction
    • Endothelin

  • Factors increasing renal blood flow

    • Dopamine - increases renal blood flow.
    • Prostaglandins -> increase blood flow in the cortex.
    • Bradykinin
    • Acetylcholine - renal vasodilation
    • Blood glucose
    • High protein diet (and amino acids) -> increases renal blood flow.
    • Atrial and brain natriuretic peptide (ANP and BNP) decrease arteriolar resistance and increase renal blood flow Source
