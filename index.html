Acute liver failure

[!INFO] Definition of acute liver failure
Presence of

  • Acute liver injury
  • Encephalopathy
  • Elevated INR
    in a patient with no prior history of liver disease.

Hepatic encephalopathy

Pathogenesis

Portal encephalopathy occurs in patients with portal to systemic shunting.
Examples: Cirrhosis, TIPS, or surgical shunts.

The synergistic action of ammonia with other toxins may account for many of the abnormalities occurring in liver failure, such as the changes in blood-to-brain transport of neurotransmitter precursors, the metabolism of amino acid neurotransmitters, and cerebral glucose oxidation. These changes may lead to activation of inhibitory (gamma-aminobutyric acid, serotonin) and impairment of excitatory (glutamate, catecholamines) neurotransmitter systems.

Increased aromatic amino acid to branch chained amino acids in plasma may contribute to hepatic encephalopathy. However, benefit or oral or IV branch chain amino acids is not clear.

Where does the ammonia come from?

  • The gastrointestinal tract is the primary source of ammonia.
  • It is produced by enterocytes by metabolism of glutamine
  • And by colonic bacterial catabolism of nitrogen containing molecules like protein and secreted urea.

Causes Precipitating Encephalopathy:

  • Infection
  • UGI bleed
  • Hypokalemia and / or metabolic alkalosis
  • Renal failure
  • Hypovolemia -> increases renal ammonia production
  • Hypoxia
  • Benzodiazepine use
  • Hypoxia
  • Constipation
  • Development of HCC / vascular occlusion.

Clinical features of hepatic encephalopathy

  • There is impairment of intellect, mood and personality change.
  • Other features like confusion, agitation, slow speech etc.
  • Hyperreflexia and increased tone is a feature.
  • Fetor hepaticus : sweet smell to the breath.
  • Convulsions suggest an alternative diagnosis.
  • Asterexis, constructional apraxia, decreased mental function (impaired serial sevens test etc.)

Acute management of hepatic encephalopathy

#2020GM-NOV/Q29
Two main arms of management:

  1. Eliminate precipitating cause
  2. Reduce blood ammonia levels.
    • Lactulose, lactitol reduce absorption of ammonia from the gut by several mechanisms.
      • Lactulose is not broken down in the intestine because there is no specific enzyme for it. Therefore, it passes into the colon where it acts as osmotic particle and it's fermentation lowers colonic pH.
      • Lower pH -> NH3 -> NH4+ which is poorly absorbed.
      • Osmotic action produces diarrhoea -> increased faecal nitrogen excretion.
      • Lactulose is also a prebiotic.
    • Rifaximine - non absorbable antibiotic which ?reduces ammonia production by gut bacteria. Studies have shown that rifaximin + lactulose has better results than lactulose alone.
    • LOLA (L ornithing L aspartate) promotes ammonia metabolism.
    • Dietary protein restriction is NOT recommended.
    • Also, correct hypokalemia as hypokalemia increases renal ammonia production.

ALS (amyotrophic lateral sclerosis)

#2020GM-NOV/Q33

  • Commonest Type of of Motor neuron disease
  • Hallmark is mixture of upper and lower motor neurons signs
  • Quickly and relentlessly progressing: death in about 5 years <- neuromuscular respiratory failure
  • Ataxia is not a feature
  • Extra pyramidal features can mimic Parkinson's - tremor, bradykinesia; may precede or follow motor signs.
  • Degeneration of frontal lobe motor neurons and the corticospinal trac -> Cause the Upper motor nerve lesions
  • No objective sensory losses.
  • Non motor symptoms:
    • Labile mood - "pseudobulbar effect" - unstoppable laughing or crying; common
    • cognitive impairment <- frontotemporal dysfunction

Antivenom

#2020GM-NOV/Q32
To be used only against

  1. Cobra
  2. Kraits
  3. Russel's viper
  4. Saw Scaled viper

There is no benefit of giving antivenom for a hump nosed viper bite, green pit viper or sea snakes.

[!WARNING] VERY HIGH RISK of reaction
81% of patients get some type of reaction and 43% get a severe reaction.
Therefore, premedication with subcutaneous adrenaline is given.

Adrenaline premedication

The dose is 0.25 mg subcutaneously for adults.
0.005 ml/Kg of 1:1000 for children
Premedication with antihistamine and hydrocortisone is not recommended as they have no effect on frequency or severity of reactions to antivenom.

[!INFO] Dose
10 - 20 ampouled dilued upto 500 ml with normal saline infused over 1 hour.
For cobra and krait, usually one round of 10 ampoules is sufficient.
30 ampules can be given as first dose in severe envenomation.

When end point for antivenom therapy is reversal of coagulopathy, as judged by the WBCT. Antivenom is NOT continued just because of continuing neuropathy.
If WBCT at 6 hours post administration is not normal, repeat the antivenom dose.

  • Antivenom can be administered solely for local envenomation but ONLY for cobra bites.

There are no absolute contraindications to antivenom treatment

Source
Patients with history of reaction to equine or other animal derived sera should still be given antivenom but with premedication. Source
Routine premedication with adrenalin should not be given because resultant hypertension may cause ICH.

Types of reaction to antivenom

  1. Early anaphylactic reactions - within minutes to 3 hours.
    1. Dry cough, fever, nausea, vomiting, abdominal colic, diarrhoea and tachycardia are all features.
  2. Pyrogenic reactions: due to pyrogenic contaminants of the antivenom; occur sin 1 to 2 hours. Causes fever with chill and rigors and hypotension.
  3. Late serum sickness like reactions : several days (mean 7) after treatment.
    1. [[#Serum sickness]]
