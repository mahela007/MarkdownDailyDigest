  • Plasminogen floats around in plasma. If it finds fibrin, it binds to it.
  • It binds to fibrin in the inactivated form (plasminogen) but requires activation by tissue plasminogen activators which diffuse into the clot from the plasma.
  • Plasminogen is activated mainly while it is bound to fibrin because fibrin has receptors for both plasminongen AND plasmin activators.
  • Similarly, activated plasmin is protected from inactivation while it is bound to fibrin. If it breaks free into the plasma, it's more vulnerable to inactivation.

Preference of prescription of drugs in heart failure with reduced Ejection Fraction (HFrEF)

[!INFO] HFrEF = EF < 40%.
Source

For HFrEF, there is universal agreement to initiate quadruple therapy as soon as possible, but the best sequencing strategy remains elusive. Source


ARNI (Sacubitril valsatan):

  • Should be started in all patients with stable HFrEF (particularly those with NYHA II or III symptoms and who have elevated BNP levels before treatment).
  • Because neprilysin inhibitors increase BNP levels, NTproBNP levels (which are not increased by the drug) should be used instead to help diagnose and manage HF.
  • Patients with chronic HFrEF who are stable on ACEi or ARB should be switched to ARNI Source <- looks like a first line drug.

Cardiac resynchronization therapy VS. Implantable cardioverter defibrillator
  • Hearts in heart failure can show impairment of coordinated contraction of the left ventcile which further impairs cardiac output.
  • This can be seen as broad QRS complexes. The broad QRS may also be due to LBBB in background of ventricular failure.
  • In both situations (i.e patients with broad QRS coplexes) patients are candidates for cardiac resynchronization therapy - (which is basically a pacemaker) if heart failure is refractory to medical management.
Implantable cardioverter defibrillators:
  • To prevent sudden cardiac death due to ventriciular arrhtymmias, because that's a common cause of death in patients with heart failure.

Haemolytic disease of the newborn

[!TIP] Mnemonic: IgG - "Goes" across the placenta.

Two forms of HDN:

  • ABO incompatibility
    • ABO antigens are inheritenced in codominant fashion.
    • Therefore, foetus can have antigens which are absent in maternal circulation and are therefore immunogenic to the mother.
  • Rh Incompatibility
  • ABO system antibodies are IgM, Rh System antibodies are IgG and only IgG crosses the placenta in significant quantities.
    • Blood type A and B mothers have IgM based antibodies.
    • However, blood type O mothers also have IgG antibodies based AB antibodies which can cross the placenta.
    • Therefore, HDN occurs mainly in O type mothers. Even so, this is uncommon as high IgG titers are needed (and this is uncommon in O mothers) and non RBC foetal tissues have A and B antigens which mob up IgG which does cross.
  • Rh incompatibility causes severe disease because anti Rh antibodies are IgG antibodies which cross freely.
