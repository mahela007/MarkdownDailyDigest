[!INFO] Anatomy of heart valves

  • The aortic valve has three cusps: the left coronary cusp (LCC), the right coronary cusp (RCC), and the non-coronary cusp (NCC).
  • The pulmonary valve has three cusps: the anterior cusp (AC), the left cusp (LC), and the right cusp (RC).
  • The mitral valve has an alphanumeric nomenclature that numbers from the anterior to the posterior, with respect to the heart, and attaches an A or a P in front of the anterior or posterior leaflets, respectively (A1-A3, P1-P3).
  • The tricuspid valve has three leaflets named the anterior (A), septal (S), and posterior (P).
  • Ejection sounds are high pitch sounds occurring in early systole. (i.e After S1)
  • They can be sign of anormal aortic / pumonary valves. - probably due to 'doming' of the valves.
  • In the absense of valve abnormality, they can be caused by abnormalities of the proximal segments of the aorta or pumonary artery.
    -Aortic ejection sound:
    • Best heart at the cardiac apex! (transmitted widely)
    • Very similar to a split S1!
    • Intensity does not vary with respriation.
    • Pathophysiology: deformed but mobile aortic valve with aortic root dilation - (intensity reduced when aortic valve is calcified).
    • Seen in bicuspid aortic valve.
    • Present in aortic stenosis AND aortic regurgitation and ascending aortic aneurysm. Can also be present in systemic hypertension.
    • Usually absent in supravalvular and subvalvular stenosis - therefore, helps to localize the site of stenosis to the valve.

Pulmonary ejection sound

  • Occurs slightly earlier than an aortic ejection click.
  • Heard over the pulmonary area only.
  • A defining features is the reduction / disappearance during inspiration.

With inspiration, the increased venous return to the RV augments the effect of right atrial systole and causes partial opening of the pulmonary valve prior to ventricular systole.

  • Causes: deformed pulmonary valve, pulmonary artery dilation, pulmonary valve stenosis, idiopathic dilation of the pulmonary artery, chronic pulmonary artery hypertension.
  • With increasing severity of stenosis, the pulmonary ejection click occurs sooner and sooner after S1.

Resistant hypertension

#2022SBR-MAY Q10
There's a pretty rigorous definition:

BP which remains above goal despite use of 3 antihypertensive agents, given at maximal doses, one of which is a diuretic.

Patients with resistant hypertension are more likely to have secondary causes. (which may contribute atleast partly).

Clinical features suggesting presence of secondary hypertension:(i.e when to evaluate patients further)

  • Onset below 30 years old
  • Resistant hypertension
  • Malignant hypertension (HT with end organ damage)
  • Acute rise / lability of blood pressure.
  • Hypertension with electrolyte / acid base abnormalities.

Causes of resistant hypertension:

There is a large overlap with 'causes of secondary hypertension'.

  1. renovascular
  2. Sleep apnoea
  3. Primary aldosteronism
  4. Primary kidney disease
  5. OCP
  6. phaeochromocytoma
  7. Cushings
  8. Coarctation of the aorta

Renovascular hypertension

Renal artery stenosis
One of the commonest correctable causes of secondary hypertension.
Clinical features:

  1. patient will have other features of atherosclerotic disease (coronary, aortic or peripheral arterial)
  2. Onset of severe hypertension (>180/120) after 55 years of age.
  3. Unexplained deterioration of kidney function during antihypertensive therapy (esp. with ACEi)
  4. Unilateral small kidney seen on imaging.
  5. Systolic-diastolic abdominal bruit that lateralizes to one side. (specificity almost 99% but sensitiviy is around 40%)
    1. auscultation should be performed in the epigastrium and all four abdominal quadrants.

How to confirm the diagnosis?

  • duplex Doppler ultrasonography is the preferred initial noninvasive diagnostic test
  • CTA
  • MRA
  • conventional renal arteriography;
    Fibromuscular dysplasia can result in intrarenal narrowing of the renal arteries. Can be missed on non-invasive imaging.

Klinefelter syndrome

  • XXY genotype. ("Male with an extra X chromosome")
  • Newborns with XXY appear normal with normal male genitalia. Differences are noticed after puberty.
  • Usually diagnosed at investigation for male infertility / gynaecomastia (patients have increased risk of breast cancer). They have "pea sized, firm testes".
  • Features: See image above; tall stature due to delayed epiphyseal closure;
  • Caused by maternal or paternal non dysjunction of chromosomes.
  • Associations: DM, Breast cancer, emphysema, bronchiectasis, learning disability

Kallmann's syndrome

Isolated GnRH deficiency.
Associated features: Anosmia, color blindness, cleft palate.
Usually X linked.
Fertility is possible with gonadotrophin therapy.
No tall stature, gynaecomastia like Klinefelter syndrome.
