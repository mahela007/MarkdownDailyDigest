[!INFO] Physiology of ADH
ADH secretion from the posterior pituitary is stimulated by

  1. osmotic stimuli
  2. Volume stimuli

    Volume stimuli override osmotic stimuli in the control of vasopressin secretion
    i.e the body will accept becoming hypotonic if it means restoring normal circulating volume.
    ADH acts on
  3. V2 receptors -> Rapid translocation of aquaporin channels in collecting duct cell vesicles to the cell membranes -> increased collecting duct permeability to water.
  4. V1a receptors -> mediate the potent vasoconstrictor activity of ADH.
  5. V1b receptors -> located in Anterior pituitary, potentiate the release of ACTH.

Regulation of ECF volume

The main determinant of plasma osmolality is Na.
Therefore, sodium concentration has a huge effect on ECF volume.

In dehydration from things like sweating and inadequate intake, there is no large Na loss; Therefore, dehydration -> increased ECF osmolality -> extracellular shift of fluid -> water is lost from both ICF and ECF -> volume depletion of ECF is low.

However, in disease states which cause significant sodium loss

  • (GI -> diarrhoea
  • Urine -> severe acidosis or adrenal insufficiency
    both sodium and water are lost -> no fluid shifts -> large volume depletion of ECF.

The primary method of volume regulation is by activation of the RAAS system; however, other systems also act to reduce Na excretion.

[!INFO] Illustration of the above:
Aldosterone increases from supine to standing reposition.
Na excretion also drops from supine to standing position.
(Both are MCQ points)
However, the change in Na excretion is too rapid to explain by aldosterone action. So it's probably due to things like acute reduction in ANP level. - Ganong.

Rheumatic fever pathology

Rheumatic carditis:

Pathogenesis:

Cross reactivity between antibodies against streptococci and myocardium causes complement activation and tissue damage.
This take 2 - 3 weeks after strep infection -> that's when symptoms appear.
Most acute infections are in children.
Few in adults -> it presents as arthritis.

Morphologic features of acute rheumatic carditis:

  • Pancarditis; Pericardium, myocardium and endocardium are all affected.
    • Aschoff bodies, collections of T lymphocytes with central necrosis are present in all 3 layers. They contain Anitschkow cells which are T lymphocytes with 'caterpillar shaped' nucleoli.
  • Pericardium - Fibrinous exudate seen.
  • Myocardium - Aschoff bodies seen in the connective tissue.
  • Endocardium - i.e valves -> vegetations seen along the valve margins.

Morphologic features of chronic rheumatic carditis

  • Aschoff bodies are replaced by fibrous scar tissue
  • Thickening and fusion of mitral valves along with thickening, shortening and fusion of chordae tendineae.
  • There is neovascularization and diffuse fibrosis.
  • Valves affected -> Mitral >>> aortic >> tricuspid > pulmonary (almost never)
  • MacCullum plaques are very rare, map like areas of thickening and roughening on the left atrial endocardium, seen in rheumatic heart disease. Source
