In the professor's opinion, trying to distinguish between vertigo and dizziness in order to sort out the diagnosis isn't useful. Many cardiac patients actually complain of "virtigo". But for (unrealistic) exam purposes the distinction is important.

The classical distinctions are

initial diagnosis

"Looking at eye movements is the key"

Left beating nystagmus means a right sided lesion.

HINTs
Vestibular tests which look at the eye movements:

  1. Head impulse test
  2. Nystagmus
  3. Tests of skew

HiNTs is for suspected stroke. Don't do it for episodic triggered vertigo. (I.e suspected BPPV)

Look for spontaneous nystagmus.
Then look for nystagmus with gaze in different directions.

With vestibular nystagmus, the nystagmus is mich worse when looking to the healthy side and becomes smaller when looking towards the affected side. The nystagmus won't change direction and it's mainly horizontal.

If the direction of nystagmus is reversed upon looking in a particular direction, the patient is likely to have a stroke becuase the vestibular pathways and thr gaze holding pathways are affected. (Or even in cerebellar ataxia).

However, even if nystagmus doesn't change direction, it could still be a stroke!
So step 2 is the head impulse test.

See videos by Peter johns

How to do the HIT:
Deviate the head away from midline by about 20 degrees and ask the patient to fixate on one point like your nose. Then flick the head back to the midline. The flick has to be fast enough that visual tracking alone won't be sufficient to fix the eyes.

With an intact VO reflex, the eye will remain "fixed dead on the target".

If the VO is impaired, the eyes will be dragged along with the head movement followed by a saccade, to get the the eyes back on point.

With left nystsgmus, during the subsequent HIT, the eyes will drift off to the right when the head is flicked back after initial left deviation and the saccade will be back to the left.
So, the saccade will be in the same direction as the initial nystsgmus.

A finding of a nystgamus with negative head impulse test is a alarming finding.
The diagnosis is usually cerebellar or brainstem stroke.

[!NOTE] Having normal head impulse test
in a healthy person is normal but having a normal HIT in a patient with nystagmus is an alarm finding!

Final step in evaluation:
Check for eye alignment with the alternate cover tests and check for vertical movement. If vertical movement is seen, it's called a skew deviation. That means there's probably a stroke! (Vertical misaligned is rare in the population)
Horizon misalignment is common in the population and doesn't have much diagnostic value.

These methods have been found to be more than 95% sensitive and specific.

MRIs miss 20% of posterior fossa stroke in the first 24-48 hours. Maximum sensitivity of MRI is at about 72 hours.
Sensitivity of CT is only like 15%.

Also look for dysarthria, diplopia, dysmetria, dysphonia -> the deadly Ds.
But the majority of strokes causing acute vestibular syndrome (like 80%) present without any of these.
Vestibular strokes also have worsening of nystagmus with movement.

The degree of ataxia (esp. truncal) correspond very will the diagnosis of stroke. More severe ataxia means stroke.. but not all stroke will have ataxia.

The pathophysiology of bronchiectasis has been poorly understoodSource

Many patients with bronchiectasis experience frequent acute worsening of symptoms that are referred to as exacerbations. Exacerbation of bronchiectasis is a major adverse event in the natural history of this disease.

Frequency of exacerbations predicts mortality
Source

Features of an exacerbation:

Bacterial infection is traditionally regarded as the most probable cause of bronchiectasis exacerbation. It is thought that alterations in the composition of the bacterial population lead to exacerbations, rather than infection with new pathogens.
Details below:
Staph. Aureus and Pseudomonas are common pathogens.

Studies don't show a significant difference in the bacterial species found during baseline disease and during an exacerbation.

"It is likely, therefore, if bacteria are involved in exacerbations that it is changes in the composition of the resident microbes of the lung (like overgrowth of pseudomonas), rather than invasion of a new pathogen, that is seen in the majority of exacerbations."

Viral infections can also trigger exacerbations. They may be the inciting event that alters the bacterial population as described above but study results are conflicting.
"The most common viruses found in patients experiencing exacerbations include rhinovirus, influenza virus A/B and seasonal coronaviruses in these studies from China."

risk factors for frequent exacerbations

  • previous exacerbations
  • chronic pseudomonas infections
  • comorbid airway diseases

management of exacerbations

Antibiotics are the mainstay treatment for bronchiectasis exacerbations and are also recommended by the international bronchiectasis guidelines.

Antibiotic duration: 14 days based on expert opinion (without high quality evidence), especially if pseudomonas is found. Shorter courses can be used based on patient response.

Curbsiders on Insulin therapy

In type 2 diabete, by the time of diagnosis about half of beta cell function is lost.
Usually if HbA1C is above 10%, insulin is a good choice.
GLP1 agonists are good drugs (considered as second line after Metformin) because they prevent diabetes associated organ dysfunction in other ways than just by reducing glucose. However, they are expensive.
They have organ benefits, mortality benefits and weight loss benefits.
As opposed to sulfonylureas which only lower sugar levels, without the other benefits.

How to start insulin

For type 2 DM, the first line would actually be basal insulin.
The body produces insulin to match the livers sugar output.

  • For type 2 diabetes, basal bolus is NOT the go to regime. Start with just basal insulin. (Need to check this)
    • eg Glargine - once daily dose. (There are also bi daily dosing types of basal insulins - determir and levemir)
    • NPH also is used as a basal insulin. But it has a peak in it's action - unlike the previous two. Peak in 6 hours. Duration 12 hours.
      • "Insulatard" is NPH insulin
      • with NPH, 2/3 of the requirement is given in the morning (so that meals will coincide with the peak) and 1/3 before dinner (eg 15 mane, 5 vesper)
    • pre-mixed inulin
      • 70:30 - 70% is NPH, 30% soluble.
      • should be given twice daily.

  • basal insulin production for a healthy human is about 1 unit/hr

Short overview of the role of the liver in glucose homeostasis

Glycogen is the storage form of carbohydrates in mammals. In humans the majority of glycogen is stored in skeletal muscles (∼500 g) and the liver (∼100 g).

The liver and muscles store glycogen. The liver continuously releases and stores glucose from / to it's glycogen depot to maintain blood glucose levels at rest.
The primary importance of this is to feed the brain which consumes about 60% of blood glucose at rest.
If the hepatic glycogen drops below normal, the liver resorts to gluconeogenesis to produce glucose. (But this is slow)

During exercise, the rapid excess demand for glucose is supplied by the breakdown of muscle glycogen.

Mechanism of SBP

Based on Curious clinicians SBP episode ->

[!INFO] Summary of mechanism of SBP
Mechanism:
Bacterially overgrowth -> translocation to the lymph nodes -> bacteraemia -> seeding of the Ascites.
Peritoneal fluid is deficient in opsonins and other stuff that inhibits bacterial growth.

Take home points:

  1. Infection at any site can predispose to SBP in a cirrhotic patient.
  2. Simply having peritoneal fluid (like in Budd-chiari) isn't sufficient for development of SBP. The other physiological alterations produced by cirrhosis are necessary.
  3. Nephrotic syndrome in children can cause SBP
  4. Anaerobic cover isn't needed for SBP prophylaxis because anaerobes aren't involved in pathogenesis.
  5. Patients with upper GI bleed are at increased risk of SBP

3 factors predisposed to SBP in cirrhosis:

  1. Small Intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO)

    1. Somehow, intestinal bacterial overgrowth occurs in cirrhosis and degree of overgrowth corresponds with Child-Pugh score. (And it's higher in patients who have ascitic fluid)
      1. It may be due to altered gut motility due to increased glucagon
      2. Maybe PPIs increase bacterial overgrowth
      3. Norfloxacin and pentoxyphilline reduce bacterial overgrowth

  2. There is increased bacterial permeability of the bowel wall

    1. Permeability correlates with the Child-Pugh score.
    2. Probably occurs due to a disruption of the epithelial tight junctions. (Mechanism unknown)
    3. The bacteria probably don't translocate entirely transmurally. It looks like they go from the luminal cavity to the mesenteric lymph nodes. And then somehow they make it to the ascitic fluid.
    4. Anaerobes don't play a significant part in this. So we don't treat SBP with metronidazole.

  3. Altered immunity

    1. Diminished function of the Kupffer cells which okay a main role in elimination of GNB from the blood. But other mechanisms may also reduce immunity in CLD.
- Vaccination of humans (**Sri lanka uses the inactivated vaccine but live attenuated vaccine is also available**).
