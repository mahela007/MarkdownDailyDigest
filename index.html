Heart failure

#2023GM Q27

Overview of HF

[!INFO] Definition of Heart Failure

  1. Heart failure refers to a complex collection of symptoms (a syndrome) AND
  2. evidence of cardiac dysfunction as a cause for these symptoms.

Haemodynamically, the heart cannot pump enough blood to meet the needs of the body or can do so only under high filling pressures.

Features of HF


Sensitivity decreases from left to right, specificity increases from left to right.

Causes of HF

Investigations in the diagnosis of heart failure

  • Gold standard is invasive measurement of filling pressures during rest and exercise.
    • If symptoms of HF AND PCWP ≥15 mmHg at rest or ≥25 mmHg during exercise => diagnosis of HF is confirmed, regardless of LVEF.
  • Echo alone cannot be used to diagnose HF.
  • BNP alone cannot be used to diagnose HF.

BNP

BNP and NT-proBNP has high sensitivity and low specificity for heart failure.[[Statistics#Sensitivity and specificity]]

[!INFO] Physiologic role of natriuretic peptides
ANP - secreted by atria
BNP - secreted by ventciles
ProBNP is converted to active BNP and inactive NT-proBNP.
ANP and BNP secretion stimulus is high ventricular filling pressure -> therefore, increased in HF.
Effects:

  • Diuresis
  • Natriuresis
  • Hypotensive effect
  • Inhibition of RAAS
  • Inhibition of endothelin secretion
  • Inhibits systemic and renal sympathetic activity

BNP

Values < 100p pg/mL have good negative predictive value for heart failure.
In HP patient with dyspnoea - value is usually above 400 pg/mL.
values of 100 - 400 are intermediate.
Other causes of BNP elevation :

  • pulmonary embolism, cor pulmonale,
  • LV dysfunction without exacerbation,
  • LV hypertrophy
  • AF is associated with higher levels of BNP in the absence of HF
NT-proBNP

In normal person, NT-proBNP levels and BNP levels are similar.
In HF, levels of NT-proBNP rise to 4 times the level of BNP.
Cutoffs suggestive of HF increase with age.

Pitfalls of BNp and NT-proBNP
  • can be falsely elevated in renal failure.
  • Lower in obese patients .
  • Can be normal in HF with preserved EF
  • Elevated in patients on ARNIs [[#ARNI - Antiotensin receptor- Neprilysin inhibitor drugs]]

Endothelin

Endothelins are potent vasoconstrictor peptides produced by the vascular endothelium and cardiac myocytes which may contribute to the regulation of myocardial function, vascular tone, and peripheral resistance in HF.

Clostridium difficile

#2023GM Q25

line:(clostridium difficile)

One of the commonest hospital acquired infections.
transmission : Faeco-oral transmission of spores.
Dangerous particularly in the older population. Usually affects somewhat sick, elderly hospitalized patients.
Risk factors -> Age > 65, , comorbid illness, hospitalization, PPI use.
Usually causes infection after normal microbiota is disrubted by Antibiotics. So, antibiotic stewardship is very important.

(disrupiton of normal microbiota can cause elevated sugar in the gut -> can cause an osmotic diarrhoea as well but patient probably won't have other features of C. difficile colitis in this case.)

The bacterium itself usually doesn't invade host tissues. But it produces two exotoxins which cause colitis. (toxin B is much more potent than toxin A)

Commonest implicated antibiotics:

[!TIP] Mnemonic : F*ckers cling on with their cephalic parts

  1. Fluoroquinolones
  2. Clindamycin
  3. Cephalosporins
  4. Penicillins

Some forms of C. difficile are commensal - won't produce toxin.
Only the toxin producing ones cause diarrhoea -> [[#Confirming clinical diagnosis|so we have to isolate the toxin for diagnosis.]]

Spectrum of clinical manifestetations:

  1. Non severe CDI - few episodes of diarrhoea with abdominal pain and leukocytosis.

  2. Severe CDI - severe lower abdominal pain, distention, fever, hypovolemia, lactic acidosis, leukocytosis > 15,000

  3. Fulminant colitis : hypotension, shock, ileus, megacolon, perforation

    Diarrhoea is usually watery. Bleeding is uncommon.

Endoscopy: Spectrum from mild erythema and friability to severe pseudomembranous colitis (that is severe inflammation of the inner lining of the bowel)

Confirming clinical diagnosis

Asymptomatic patients should not be screened for C diff as

  1. Some strains are not toxigenic
  2. Even toxin producing strains could be colonizers.
  3. Conversely, even healthy patients with colonization can have toxin producing bacteria.
  4. Therefore, only send samples from patients who have the clinical picture of CDI.
    1. Liquid stool samples are sent.
    2. NAAT
    3. Enzyme immunoassay for C. difficile GDH
    4. Enzyme immunoassay for C. difficile toxins A and B
    5. Cell culture cytotoxicity assay
    6. Selective anaerobic culture - from rectal swab

management

  • Stop offending antibiotics
  • Infection control
  • Nutrtion and fluid management
  • If suggestive of fulminant CDI
    • Oral vancomycin - only indication for oral vancomycin
    • IV metronidazole
    • Faecal microbiota transplantation
    • +/- surgical opinion
    • Fidaxomicin can also be used.

They are mainly bacteriostatic.

BUT both oral and IV erythromycin have significant side effects - so clarithromycin is used (having less SE but similar specturm. )
