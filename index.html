Acute interstitial nephritis

#2020SBR-NOV/Q30

  • Characterized by inflammatory infiltrate in the renal interstitium.
    • i.e inflammation of parts of parts of the kidney other than the glomeruli
  • Caused by antibiotics (and other drugs), autoimmune disease like sarcoidosis, infections and TINU (tubulerinterstitial nephritis with uveitis) syndrome.
  • Drugs: NSAIDS, cephalosporins, PPIs and many other antibiotics.
  • Infections; legionella, leptospira, CMV and streptococcus.
  • Autoimmune associations: Sarcoidosis, Granulomatosis with polyangitis, SLE, Sjogrens
  • Classical clinical features are fever, rash and eosinphilia.
    • Usually no significant proteinuria but can be present.
    • Can present with proteinuria if the disease is NSAID induced.
  • Investigation : Urine white cell casts, eosinophiluria (but this is not sensitive or specific). Definitive diagnosis required renal biopsy.
  • Treatment: Can progress to ESKD; early recognition and cessation of the offending drug is required. If symptoms to no resolve within a few days, corticosteroid therapy.

Prinzmetal angina

#2020SBR-NOV/Q01

Typical presentation

  • Very similar ECG changes to STEMI but changes are only transient.
  • Characteristics of the pain are indistinguishable from ischemic angina. Even autonomic symptoms are present. It is also relieved by nitrates. (However response is more rapid than thrombotic ischemia)
  • Spasm may be so severe that patient even develops arrhythmias / cardiac arrest.
  • Specific features:
    • Angina occurs at rest and particularly from midnight to early morning.
    • Effort and exercise tolerance are preserved but spasm can be triggered by exercise during 'hot phase' of the condition.
    • Can be precipitated by hyperventilation.
      • Other triggers: recreational drugs and alcohol, Exposure to extreme cold, allergic reactions, magnesium deficiency.
    • Episodes can appear in clusters

Takotusubo cardiomyopathy

#2020SBR-NOV/Q01

[!TIP] Summary
Mimics STEMI clinically, electrically and biochemically. ST-elevations seen. Troponins are positive! (but ? modestly)
Causes by transient stress induced LV systolic dysfunction. Systolic murmur is present.
Causes heart failure, VF and VT.

Suspect in elderly women presenting with ACT.

  • AKA stress cardiomyopathy; apical ballooning syndrome, takotsubo cardiomyopathy, broken heart syndrome, and stress-induced cardiomyopathy.

  • There is transient regional systolic dysfunction, mimicking the dysfunction after an MI. Predominantly affect midcavity and apex of the left ventricle. Frequently extends beyond the territory of a single coronary artery.

  • 80-90% of patients are women and mean age is around 66 (i.e elderly).

  • Pathogenesis: Postulated mechanisms are catecholamine excess, microvascular dysfunction coronary artery spasm.

  • Clinical picture: Triggered by intense emotional or physical stress.

    • Physical triggers: post surgery, acute respiratory failure, fracture, infection
    • Psychological triggers: grief, panic, interpersonal conflict etc
    • Commonest symptoms: Chest pain, dyspnoea, syncope. They can develop more severe features like tachyarrhytmias (VF and VT), signs of heart failure and mitral regurgitation and hypotention.
    • There is LV outflow tract obstruction caused by LV basal hyperkinesis -> causes and late peaking systolic murmur like HOCM.
    • ST elevation in anterior leads is common on ECG. ST depression is less common. Q waves and T inversions are seen.
    • Troponins are +ve.

[!INFO] Possible diagnostic criteria (indicative of the clinical picture)

  • Transient LV systolic dysfunction
  • Absence of obstructive coronary disease or angiographic evidence of acute plaque rupture in the relevant territory!
  • New electrocardiographic abnormalities (either ST-segment elevation and/or T wave inversion) or modest elevation in cardiac troponin.
  • Absence of pheochromocytoma or myocarditis.

  • Thus, a diagnosis of stress cardiomyopathy generally requires an electrocardiogram, cardiac troponin levels, coronary angiography, and serial assessment of LV systolic function.

Dyslipidaemia / lipid lowering

Summary of apolipoproteins and their roles

Apoprotein Location Role
Apo B48 Chylomicrons Signature molecule of chylomicrons
Apo B100 VLDL, IDL and LDL Signature molecule of endogenous lipids; used for liver uptake of LDL
Apo A1 HDL Signature molecule of HDL; recycled upon hepatic uptake of HDL particles
Apo E HDL and VLDL/IDL/LDL Exchanged between various lipid particles; receptor for liver uptake
Apo C2 VLDL/IDL/LDL, HDL Binds to receptors in organs allowing them to extract lipids from the particles
Apo C3 Same as above Delays clearance of particles

Summary of enzymes involved

HMG-CoA reductase: Rate limiting enzyme in choleseterol sythesis : found in the liver and ? other tissues
LCAT : the enzyme synthesized by the liver and attached to HDL which exterifies cholesterol so that it becomes trapped inside HDL. Required for reverse transport of cholesterol.
LPL: lipoprotein lipase: the enzyme found on tissues which removes lipids from VLDL and it's derivatives, increasing their density.

  1. Disorders of chylomicrons and VLDL -

    1. They cause HYPERTRIGLYCERIDAEMIA
    2. commonest - polygenic hypertriglyceridaemia. ("vast majority" of hypertriglyceridaemia patients are polygenic.)
    3. lipoprotein lipase deficiencies - accumulation of chylomicrons in blood -> massive hyperlipidaemia (can see lipid layer in serum samples)

  2. disorders of LDL -

    1. They cause HYPERCHOLESTEROLAEMIA

    2. Heterozygous familial hypercholesterolaemia (HeFH) - mild to severe lipid deragement (==1/1350== incidence)

    3. homozygous familial hypercholesterolaemia (HoFH) - HUGELY elevated LDL-C (==1/250,000== incidence). Most die of ischemic heart disease by age 35

    4. Mutations in the apoB and PCSK9 genes - same presentation as HeFH, but milder. Only way to differentiate it genetic tests. - relatively common

    5. Polygenic hypercholesterolaemia - no single identifiable gene deffect but have moderately elevated cholesterol levels.

      1. phenotype: ↑ TGL +/- ↑ LDL-c + suppression of HDL
  • Serum triglyceride concentration reflects VLDL concentration in a fasting sample.
  • In a fasting blood sample, chylomicrons are absent.
  • Cholesterol and HDL-C levels do not change significantly after a meal so random samples (no need for fasting) are good for screening of cholesterol levels. Source
  • If abnormalities are detected in any lipids (including TGL), fasting test should be done.
  • Lipid concentrations are reduced for 3 months following acute severe illness (inclding MI). Measure levels either within 48 hours of onset or 3 months after.

Thrombotic microangipathy

TMAs characterized by endothelial cell injury, intravascular platelet-fibrin thrombi, and vascular damage. Source

What type of patients get it?

Overview

(There is a whole heap of causes of secondary thrombotic microangiopathies - pregnancy, drugs and toxins, HIV, SLE. Always exclude these)

Clinical features

Presentation of TTP

Acute or subacute onset of the following symptoms related to central nervous system (CNS) dysfunction, anemia, or thrombocytopenia:

  • Neurologic features: Alteration in mental status, seizures, hemiplegia, paresthesias, visual disturbance, and aphasia.
  • Fatigue may accompany the anemia
  • Severe bleeding from thrombocytopenia is unusual, although petechiae are common
  • Haemolysis -> increased production -> Reticulocytosis,
  • Haemolsis -> elevated lactate dehydrogenase, unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia and low haptoglobin. - Feature of haemolysis
