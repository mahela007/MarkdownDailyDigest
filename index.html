Geriatric medicine

[!INFO] The main questions in geriatric medicine - Kumar and Clark
In Geriatric medicine, a balance has to be found between

  1. What can be done for the patient and
  2. What should be done for the patient.
    • Edmunt Pellegrino

Common problems in geriatric medicine are

  • Frailty - decreased physiological reserve of the organ systems. In a frail person, a given physiological stress will have a greater negative impact on the organ systems.

    • Frailty does not necessarily correspond with age; it is possible to be young and frail and elderly and not frail. eg: fit person -> uti -> minimal symptoms.
    • frail person -> uti -> delerium etc.
    • Therefore, a patient must be managed according to their functional status and *not their chronological age.
    • 3 or more of the criteria define the presence of frailty.

  • Sarcopenia: progressive, systemic muscle disorder which leads to loss of muscle fibers, in particular fast twitch fibers.

    • Increased risk of falls and fractures
    • Maximum amount of oxygen consumption (VO2 max) reduces.
    • Gait speed reduces
    • Reduced energy expenditure ensues.
    • This somehow causes an energy deficit (probably due to anorexia of aging)-> not enough ATP for apoptosis -> cells instead undergo necrosis -> Even more ATP is used up -> cycle propagates -> more sarcopenia -> frailty cycle

  • Multimorbidity - presence of two or more medical conditions that may or may not interact.

    • Multimorbidity can lead to prescribing cascades and co prescription (omeprazole added to aspirin therapy)

  • Disability : Physical or mental impairment which has substantial and long term negative impact on the ability of a person to carry out daily activities.

Other common issues in the elderly

  • Polypharmacy
  • Falls

  • Urinary Tract infections
