  • Like ethyl alcohol and methanol, ethylene glycol is metabolized by ADH. (alcohol dehydrogenase) In this step it forms glycoaldehyde. Through interaction with aldehyde dehydrogenase, ethylene glycol is then metabolized to glycolic acid (GA), which accumulates and can cause a profound metabolic acidosis. This glycolic acid is eventually converted into glyoxylic acid, and then into the highly toxic oxalate or the safer glutamate or α-ketoadipic acid metabolites.
  • Ethylene glycol -> glycoaldehyde -> glycolid acid ( ⬇ph) -> glycoxylic acid -> oxalate.
  • Toxic dose: 200ml of 50% solution.
    - Mnemonic: CNS depression in hours -> Kidney on organ injury in next 1 to 2 days.
    - 30 mins to 12 hours : Ethanol like effects on the CNS.
    - 12 to 48 hours: metabolite (glycolic acid) produces severe high anion gap metabolic acidosis. Calcium oxalate crystals become deposited in brain, lungs kidneys and heart.
    - 24 to 72 hours, acute kidney injury can result from the direct renal toxic effects of the ethylene glycol metabolite calcium oxalate monohydrate.
  • Diagnosis
    • Causes an increased oslmolar gap. (osmoles which are unaccounted for by Sodium , glucose and urea)
    • Plasma osmolality: Posm =2 [Na(+)]+glucose (mg/dL)/18+BUN (mg/dL)//2.8
    • Causes of increased osmolar gap : Alcohol (ethanol, methanol, ethylene glycol poisoning)
    • Initial emergency department treatment includes infusion of crystalloids to enhance renal clearance of the toxic metabolites.
    • Ethyl alcohol (ethanol) has traditionally been used for antidotal treatment, but it has largely been supplanted by fomepizole in the United States.

Fatty cast:

Composed of Tamm-Horsfall protein which is made by the renal tubular epithelium.

  • Hyaline casts - only tamm horsfall protein; least significant type of cast.
  • Granular casts - consist of cell debris, plasma proteins (fibrinogen, immune complexes).
    • Course, deeply pigmented, muddy brown casts are considered characteristic of ATN.
    • Pyelonephritis
    • Glomerulonephritis
    • Presence of red cells in the cast => Glomerular injury.
  • Fatty casts / urinary lipids (cholesterol esters) : nephrotic syndrome, mercury poisoning, ethylene glycol poisoning.
  • WBC casts - interstitial nephritis, pyelonephritis.
  • Tubular epithelial cell casts : ATN, acute interstitial nephritis, proliferative glomerulonephritis.
    • "hepatic" are the commonest extrarenal manifestations - 70%.
  • Cardiovascular:
    • Mitral valve prolapse.
