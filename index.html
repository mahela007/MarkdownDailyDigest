Sickle cell disease complications


Pathogenesis : point mutation in beta globin gene. Thymine for adenine causes valine for glutamic acid. This produces "HbS".


Deoxygenated HbS polymerises insolubly. Cells progressively become rigid and irreversibly sicked due to intracellular dehytration and potassium loss.
Symptoms begin at 6 months of age when Hb syntheis switches from HbF to HbA.
HbS has a lower affinity for oxygen than HbA.

Inheritance and phenotypes:

  • HbAS (HbA gene + HbS gene) -> Asymptomatic carriers.

    • Offers protection against Falciparum malaria. Mechanism: falciparum infected cells sickle more rapidly and are removed by the spleen more quickly.Source

  • HbSS -> severe symptoms.

Coninheritance of other abnormal haemoglobins:

  • HbS + HbC = HbSC => predisposition to thrombosis and pulmonary embolism but longer life expenctancy than HbSS.
  • HbS + Beta thalassemia => splenomegaly +, lower MCV and MCH than HbSS.

Effects:

  • Shortened red cell survival
  • Impaired microcirculation and organ ischemia

Sickling is precipitated by

  • low temperature, acidosis, hypoxia, dehyration and infection.

Symptoms:

Life threatening complications

  1. Acute chest syndrome: Infection, pulmonary infarction from occlusion or fat embolism from necrotic bone. (chest pain with fever, SOB, hypoxia, with new CXR changes) (Mx is with oxygen antibiotics and exchange transfusion if poor response to initial measures).

    • Occurs in upto 50% of Sickle cell patients! Major cause of mortality!
    • Infection can be due to chlamydia, mycyplasma and strep pneumoniae.

  2. The splene in Sickle cell disease:

    • Splenomegaly is found during the first decade of life, but later it undergoes atrophy progressively due to repeated vaso-occlusive attacks, ultimately leading to autosplenectomy. (spleen becomes fibrotic)Source
    • Splenic sequestration crisis:
      • RBCs become captured and pool within the spleen and a large volume of circulating blood also gets trapped there -> hypovolemia -> chock and death!
      • Patient presents with rapildy enlarging spleen and Hb Drop (approx 2 mg /dL)
      • Life threatening complication with high mortality! and high recurrent risk; Splenctomy is done for secondary prevention.
      • Occurs in people who's spleen hasn't yet become fibrotic.
      • Increased splenic size is a risk factors as is Parvovirus B19 infection.

  3. Aplastic crisis:

    • Acute drop in Hb with reduced reticulocytes.
    • Commonly due to parvovirus B19 with infects erythrocyte precursors.
    • In SCD patients who have decreased erythropoietic reserve, this can be life threatening.
    • Usually Erythyropoiesis recovers in about a week.

Other complications

  • Vasoocclusive crises: Dactylitis and acute bone pain.

  • Pulmonary hypertension: Probably multifactorial etiology including sickle cell vasculopathy.

  • Most common cause of death is pulmonary hypertension and chronic lung disease.
  • Anaemia: usually Hb is low but stable. Splenic sequestration and bone marrow aplasia can cause sudden drops.
  • Bone: Avascular necrosis of hips and shoulders, osteomyelitis with staph, strep and salmonella.
  • infections in occluded tissues (bones, lungs and kidneys)
  • Leg ulcers
  • Cardiomegaly, iron overload and no atherogenic MIs (vasoocclusive)
  • Neurologic : TIA, fit, infarction, ICH, cognitive impairment due to microinfarcts.
  • Renal: chronic tubulointerstitial nephritis and renal papillary necrosis.
  • Cholelithiasis
  • Hepatomegaly from RBC trapping.
  • priapism
  • Spontaneous abortion (impaired placental blood flow)
  • Various eye complications.
  • Functional asplenia occurs in all
  • Bowel ischaemia

Diagnosis:
Diagnostic test is Hb electrophoresis: no HbA, 90-95% HbS and 2-20% HbF.

Management:
Crises: Extreme pain -> morphine. Oxygen and hydration. Antibiotics if needed.
Pneumococcal, meninococcal and HIb vaccines due to asplenia.
Blood transfusion for anaemia -> goal is to suppress endogenous erythropoiesis to keep HbS < 30% and Hb < 100;
Hydroxycarbamide (hydroxyurea) works by increases HbF levels; given to all patients.
Stem cell transplant can be used.

Benign gastric polyps
75% of gastric polyps are hyperplastic or inflammatory, arising due to chronic gastritis. Risk of dysplasia increases with polyp size. 10% of polyps turn out to be gastric adenomas.

Presentation: early symptoms mimic GORD and are easy to miss.

Risk factors:

  • Mutations in CDH1 gene causing reduced levels of E-cadherin
  • APC gene mutations (adenomatous polyposis coli)
  • H pylori
  • EBV infection! (about 10% of gastric CA is associated with EBV infection.
  • Male to female -> 2:1
  • Smoking is a risk factor!

Types:
Lauren classification:

  • Intestinal type: Bulkly, glandular cancers: usually adenocarcinoma. Associated with chronic gastritis and metaplasia. This type of cancer is reducing in incidence.
  • Diffuse type: infiltrative growth pattern, desmoplastic reaction gives rise to linitis plastica.
  • Commonest type is adenocarcinoma comprising 90%.
    • Commonest variants of adenocarcinoma are tubular > Papillary > Mucinous.
  • Stomach shows a particular predisposition to extranodal lymphomas.
