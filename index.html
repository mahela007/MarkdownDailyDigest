Pain physiology

#2020BSQ-NOV/Q63
Acute pain : last for the duration of the stimulus and the time required for healing.
Chronic pain: lasts beyond the time required for healing.
A delta fibers sharp - immediate pain..
C fibers - diffuse, dull pain.
They are stimulated by chemicals released following injury.

Afferent pain fibers send impulses are mediated by glutamate and substance P.
Pain stimuli are transmitted to the thalamus and limbic system via the spinothalamic (for precise localization of pain) and the spinoreticular tract (for emotional association of pain).

Transmission of pain impulses is modulated by monoaminergic descending pathways.

Gate theory of pain


The the proximal progression of pain impulses is modulated as the first synapse by

  • Afferent non-nociceptive large diameter fibers.
    • This phenomenon is used in TENS treatment of pain.
  • Descending pathways in the periaqueductal gray matter.
    Similar modulation also occurs in the brain stem under influence of the thalamic and limbic system.

Sensitization: when damaged tissue becomes hypersensitive and normal stimuli become painful. This is a protective mechanism. Also called allodynia.

Neuropathic pain results from damage to the nociceptive neurons. (eg radiculopathy, post stroke pain and post herpetic neuralgia).

Neurotransmitters involved in transmitting pain:

  • Excitatory: Glutamate, Substance P and CGRP.
  • Inhibitory: GABA
  • Opioid receptors: Stimulated by endorphins or opioids. They inhibit pain. Found in the spinal cord and PAG.
    • 3 Types of opioid receptors: μ-mu, δ-delta and κ-kappa distributed widely through the CNS.
    • Endogenous opioid peptides which stimulate these are
      • μ-mu -endorphins
      • δ-delta - encephalins
      • and κ-kappa - dynorphins
    • These modulate pain transmission by inducing hyperpolarization of neurons or blocking neurotransmitter release. Source
  • Noradrenaline and 5-HT (Serotonin) are found in descending inhibitory pathways.
  • Vanilloid receptor is activated by capsaicin (producing pain).
  • Other substances involved in pain
    • Nerve growth factor : a key element of inflammatory pain. It induces hyperalgesia by up-regulating the transcription of genes encoding receptors, ion channels, and neuropeptides. Source

Meta-analysis

  • A meta-analysis (MTA) analyses calculates the the direction and size of the 'effect' over all relevant studies, published AND unpublished.
  • It's better than a qualitative summary when it comes to analysing multiple studies.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Very time consuming. It may aggregate studies which are too dissimilar.
    • It may ignore the quality of some of the studies it analyses. (i.e low quality studies may affect the result)
  • It's used to answer a research research question by summarizing quantitative findings from studies.
  • Any meta analysis can focus on only one metric at a time.
  • Eligibility criteria: to select relavant topics:
    • Criteria should ensure that selected studies are sufficiently similar in topic and methadology.
    • eg : Participant characteristics, variables included, language, date range, type of studies (peer review / not) and type of experimental design.
  • Literature search and review:
    • At this stage, identify, retrieve and review selected studies. The goal is to be exhaustive but this can be very time consuming.
    • Search through databases, search reference lists, conference proceedings, contact authors etc.
  • Once studies are identified, they must be compared against the exclusion criteria.
    • Ideally 2 people (double coded) should review a study for inclusion or exclusion.
    • This process should be rigorously documented.
  • Then effect size for each study is calculated so that they can be aggregated later.
    • Effect size metrics include : Cohen's d, (group differences) and Pearson's r (correlation between two variables)
    • Standard error (which indicates precision) must also be calculated.
  • Analysis

The effect size and precision of each individual study are aggregated into a summary statistic, which can be done with meta-analysis software. Researchers should confirm that the effect sizes are independent of each other (i.e., no overlap in participants). Additionally, researchers must select either a fixed effects model (i.e., assumes all studies share one true effect size) or a random effects model (i.e., assumes the true effect size varies among studies). The random effects model is typically preferred when the studies have been conducted using different methodologies.

During analysis, the effect sizes of the included studies are weighted by their precision (e.g., inverse of the sampling error variance) and the mean is calculated.

The mean effect size represents the direction and/or magnitude of the effect summarized across all eligible studies accompanied by a confidence interval (measure of the precision) and a p value
Forrest plots are commonly used to display meta-analyis results.

Heterogeneity: Measure of variation among effect sizes in the included studies:
Researchers can quantify heterogeneity (e.g., Q, _t2, I_2), which is a measure of the variation among the effect sizes of included studies
Moderator variables, such as the quality of the studies or age of participants, may be included to examine sources of heterogeneity.
Because published studies may be biased towards significant effects, it is important to evaluate the impact of publication bias (e.g., funnel plot, Rosenthal’s Fail-safe N). Sensitivity analysis can indicate how the results of the meta-analysis would change if one study were excluded from the analysis.

  1. Publication Bias: Studies with positive results are more likely to be published, leading to an overestimation of treatment effects.

  2. Selection Bias: Inclusion of studies that are not representative of the entire body of research on a topic can skew results.

  3. Reporting Bias: Selective reporting of outcomes or failure to report negative findings can distort overall effect estimates.

  4. Language Bias: Restricting inclusion to studies in a specific language can introduce bias.

  5. Time-Lag Bias: Delayed publication of studies with positive results can affect the outcome.

  6. Sponsorship Bias: Studies funded by industry interests may have biased results, influencing the overall analysis.

  7. Outcome Reporting Bias: Selectively reporting outcomes within a study can impact meta-analysis results.

  8. Duplicate Publication Bias: Including the same study multiple times in an analysis can skew results.

  9. Methodological Quality Bias: Lower-quality studies may disproportionately influence the meta-analysis.

Meta-analysts employ various methods to detect and address these biases, such as sensitivity analyses and statistical adjustments, to ensure the validity of their conclusions.

Lynch syndrome

[!TIP] overall summary
Granuloma formation is a histologic pattern of chronic inflammation.
Macrophages and tissue macrophages (histiocytes) usually form granulomas in order to eliminate an antigen which cannot be removed by a single macrophage.

  • However, sometimes, granulomas are formed in the absense of an antigen, due to autoimmune diseases.
    When a macrophage encounters something it cannot phagocytose, it secretes mediators (?cytokines) which promote migration of other macrophages to the site.
    When many macrophages arrive at the site, they collectively undergo changes which allow them to form functional granulomas:
  1. Epithelialization : where the cell membranes begin to interdigitate.
  2. Giant cell formation.
  3. This is when multiple macrophages fuse to form a giant cell with multiple nuclei.
    In addition, other types of immune cells are also recruited.

The type of immune cell that is recruited and the cytokines secreted by it will determine how the granuloma 'matures'.

Specially, Th cells have a significant role to play here.
When some granulomas mature, the central macrophages undergo necrosis ->

  • This is called a necrotizing granuloma and is charachteristic of certain diseases.
    Other granulomas will undergo fibrosis. Also characteristic of some diseases.
    The main patterns that we are concerned with are caseous vs non caseous necrosis and necrotizing vs. non nectrotizing granulomas.
