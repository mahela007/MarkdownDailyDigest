Significance of ST elevation in lead aVR

Lead aVR is more useful when it's inverted => -aVR.
$$
aVR\times-1 = -aVR
$$
-aVR looks at the heart from a view between I and II.

  • Normal appearance: aVR looks like an inverted lead II. All waves are inverted.
  • Therefore, in -aVR, the deflections are upright.
    Mathematically, it's a graph of the voltage of the right arm with respect to the average of the left arm and left leg.

Causes for ST elevation in aVR

  1. Diffuse sub endocardial ischemia where it is due to reciprocal changes of st depression in I, II, aVL and V4 - V6.
  2. Infarction of the basal septum, i.e. a STEMI involving aVR
  • like Wellen's syndrome.
    • Peaked T - De Winter

Posterior myocardial infarction

[!WARNING] ST depressions can indicate a STEMI!
If they are in leads V1-V3

LITFL:
Following changes in V1 to V3:

  • Horizontal ST depression

  • Tall, broad R waves (>30ms)

  • Upright T waves

  • Dominant R wave (R/S ratio > 1) in V2

  • Posterior MI commonly occurs as a complication or extension of acute inferior STEMI.

Right ventricular infarction

  • Suspect and look for right ventricular infarction in all patients with inferior STEMI
    Suspect when:
  • ST elevation in V1
  • ST elevation in V1 and ST depression in V2 (highly specific for RV infarction)
  • Isoelectric ST segment in V1 with marked ST depression in V2
  • ST elevation in III > II (Lead III is more rightward facing than lead II and hence more sensitive to the injury current produced by the right ventricle)

Staging


The stages are as follows:

  1. Stage 1: The skin is intact with nonblanchable erythema.
  2. Stage 2: There is partial-thickness skin loss involving the epidermis and dermis.
  3. Stage 3: A full-thickness loss of skin extends to the subcutaneous tissue but does not cross the fascia beneath it. Slough or eschar may be visible, and the lesion may be foul-smelling.
  4. Stage 4: Full-thickness skin loss extends through the fascia with considerable tissue loss. There may be muscle, bone, tendon, or joint involvement.
  5. Unstageable: The depth is unknown because slough or eschar obscures the extent of tissue damage.

    UTIs include
  1. cystitis - bladder and lower urinary tract infection
  2. pyelonephritis - upper urinary tract and kidneys.

Complicated UTI : A UTI with infection ascending above the bladder -

  1. i.e a UTI with systemic symptoms
  2. a UTI with pyelonephritis
  3. a UTI with sepsis / bacteraemia
  4. Pelvic or perineal pain in men which may indicate prostatitis.

Acute simple cystitis:
Syptoms of cystis in the absence of any of the above.

Asymptomatic Bacteuria :
The presence of bacteuria without even the symptoms of cystitis.

[!WARNING] Pregnant women and renal transplant recipients are special cases
They should be managed differently.

Is a spectrum ranging from asymptomatic gallstones to biliary colic, cholecystitis, choledocholithiasis, and cholangitis.

Most patients with gall stones (80% cholesterol, 20% pigment) are asymptomatic.

2% of those will asymptomatic galls stones will go in to develop biliary colic when stones pass through the bile ducts.

20% of those with biliary colic will develop cholecystitis where the biliary obstruction by the gall stone becomes prolonged.(Several hours). Pathologically, this corresponds to inflammation of the gall bladder wall.

Presentation

Biliary colic
Constant (not colicky) pain lasting 1 to 5 hours.
Left hypochondriac pain+/- radiation to right scapula or back.
Onset at night, hours after a meal.

Cholecystitis
Similar to biliary colic but pain is persistent (>6 hrs). Nausea, vomiting and fever+.
patient will often give a history of similar episodes which settled spontaneously. Usually more ill appearing than simple biliary colic patients, and they usually lie still on the examination table.

Abdominal examination in both gallbladder colic and cholecystitis is remarkable for epigastric or right upper quadrant tenderness and abdominal guarding.
