Acute liver failure

[!INFO] Definition of acute liver failure
Presence of

  • Acute liver injury
  • Encephalopathy
  • Elevated INR
    in a patient with no prior history of liver disease.

Hepatic encephalopathy

Pathogenesis

Portal encephalopathy occurs in patients with portal to systemic shunting.
Examples: Cirrhosis, TIPS, or surgical shunts.

The synergistic action of ammonia with other toxins may account for many of the abnormalities occurring in liver failure, such as the changes in blood-to-brain transport of neurotransmitter precursors, the metabolism of amino acid neurotransmitters, and cerebral glucose oxidation. These changes may lead to activation of inhibitory (gamma-aminobutyric acid, serotonin) and impairment of excitatory (glutamate, catecholamines) neurotransmitter systems.

Increased aromatic amino acid to branch chained amino acids in plasma may contribute to hepatic encephalopathy. However, benefit or oral or IV branch chain amino acids is not clear.

Where does the ammonia come from?

  • The gastrointestinal tract is the primary source of ammonia.
  • It is produced by enterocytes by metabolism of glutamine
  • And by colonic bacterial catabolism of nitrogen containing molecules like protein and secreted urea.

Causes Precipitating Encephalopathy:

  • Infection
  • UGI bleed
  • Hypokalemia and / or metabolic alkalosis
  • Renal failure
  • Hypovolemia -> increases renal ammonia production
  • Hypoxia
  • Benzodiazepine use
  • Hypoxia
  • Constipation
  • Development of HCC / vascular occlusion.

Clinical features of hepatic encephalopathy

  • There is impairment of intellect, mood and personality change.
  • Other features like confusion, agitation, slow speech etc.
  • Hyperreflexia and increased tone is a feature.
  • Fetor hepaticus : sweet smell to the breath.
  • Convulsions suggest an alternative diagnosis.
  • Asterexis, constructional apraxia, decreased mental function (impaired serial sevens test etc.)

Acute management of hepatic encephalopathy

#2020GM-NOV/Q29
Two main arms of management:

  1. Eliminate precipitating cause
  2. Reduce blood ammonia levels.
    • Lactulose, lactitol reduce absorption of ammonia from the gut by several mechanisms.
      • Lactulose is not broken down in the intestine because there is no specific enzyme for it. Therefore, it passes into the colon where it acts as osmotic particle and it's fermentation lowers colonic pH.
      • Lower pH -> NH3 -> NH4+ which is poorly absorbed.
      • Osmotic action produces diarrhoea -> increased faecal nitrogen excretion.
      • Lactulose is also a prebiotic.
    • Rifaximine - non absorbable antibiotic which ?reduces ammonia production by gut bacteria. Studies have shown that rifaximin + lactulose has better results than lactulose alone.
    • LOLA (L ornithing L aspartate) promotes ammonia metabolism.
    • Dietary protein restriction is NOT recommended.
    • Also, correct hypokalemia as hypokalemia increases renal ammonia production.
