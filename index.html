Alzheimers disease

#2021GM-NOV Q01

Molecular basis

Transmembrane protein "Amyloid precursor protein" (APP) is broken down by two pathways.

  • BACE + gamma-secretase pathway -> produces Beta amyloid.
  • Alpha secretase + gamma secretase pathway -> produces harmless products.

Mutations in APP or gamma secretase increase the rate of formation of beta amyloid which causes Alzheimer's disease.

Beta amyloid accumulation is

  • directly toxic to neurons

  • Large deposits form Neuritic plaques elicit local inflammatory response.

  • Causes hyperphosphorylation of intracellular Tau protein.

    • Tau protein is a protein which stabilizes microtubules and regulates protein binding to microtubules in healthy cells.
    • Phosphorylation causes Tau protein to redistribute from axons to dendrites and cell bodies where it forms Neurofibrillary tangles which cause neuronal dysfunction and cell death.

  • So, plaques are extracellular and tangles are intracellular.

  • Neuritic plaques are collections of dystrophic neurites around a central amyloid core.

    • There is sparing of the primary motor and sensory cortices until the late stages.

  • Neurofibrillary tangles - intracellular accumulations of phosphorylated Tau protein. They are not specific to Alzheimer's dementia.

Genetic risk factors:

Risk is increased by:

  • Down syndrome (APP is on Chromosome 21)
  • Point mutations of APP gene.
  • E4 allele of apolipoprotein E gene
  • Presenilin 1 and 2 - mutations affect gamma secretase function.

other risk factors

Age, head trauma, vascular factors

Morphology

Cortical atrophy mainly in frontal, temporal and parietal lobes.
MRI -> changes visible first in mesial temporal lobe structures including hippocampi (hippocampus).
Early stage imaging may be normal.

[!INFO] Limbic System
Hippocambus:
Essentially the memory centre of our brains. Here, our episodic memories are formed and catalogued to be filed away in long-term storage across other parts of the cerebral cortex.
Amygdala : "almond like shape"
Located right next to the hippocampus, the left and right amygdalae play a central role in our emotional responses, including feelings like pleasure, fear, anxiety and anger. The amygdala also attaches emotional content to our memories, and so plays an important role in determining how robustly those memories are stored. Memories that have strong emotional meaning tend to stick.

Most cases are sporadic. Only 5-10% are familial.

[!INFO] Tau protein

Tau protein is a protein found in diverse cells like amoebas, insects to humans. It regulated the binding of other proteins to the microtubules. Source and helps to stabilize the cytoskeleton of neurons.

Many neurodegenerative diseases are characterised by tangled proteins called Tau filbrils.
