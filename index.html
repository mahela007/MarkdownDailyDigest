Rheumatic fever pathology

Rheumatic carditis:

Pathogenesis:

Cross reactivity between antibodies against streptococci and myocardium causes complement activation and tissue damage.
This take 2 - 3 weeks after strep infection -> that's when symptoms appear.
Most acute infections are in children.
Few in adults -> it presents as arthritis.

Morphologic features of acute rheumatic carditis:

  • Pancarditis; Pericardium, myocardium and endocardium are all affected.
    • Aschoff bodies, collections of T lymphocytes with central necrosis are present in all 3 layers. They contain Anitschkow cells which are T lymphocytes with 'caterpillar shaped' nucleoli.
  • Pericardium - Fibrinous exudate seen.
  • Myocardium - Aschoff bodies seen in the connective tissue.
  • Endocardium - i.e valves -> vegetations seen along the valve margins.

Morphologic features of chronic rheumatic carditis

  • Aschoff bodies are replaced by fibrous scar tissue
  • Thickening and fusion of mitral valves along with thickening, shortening and fusion of chordae tendineae.
  • There is neovascularization and diffuse fibrosis.
  • Valves affected -> Mitral >>> aortic >> tricuspid > pulmonary (almost never)
  • MacCullum plaques are very rare, map like areas of thickening and roughening on the left atrial endocardium, seen in rheumatic heart disease. Source

Active against Streptococci, Staph epidermidis and some MRSA and also most gram -ve organisms including pseudomonas.

  • 'Difficult' gram -ve pathogen.
  • It has metallo-beta lactamases which inactivate all beta lactamses including carbapenems.
  • It's sensitive to
    • pip-taz, ticarcillin
    • ciprofloxacin,
    • Antipseudomonal cephalosporins - ceftazidime and cefepime.
    • High doses of gentamicin and amikacin. (getamicin is used in pseudomonal infective endocarditis)
