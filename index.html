  • Infection occurs by ingestion of mature cysts from water or foods with faecal contamination.

  • Cysts excystation occurs in the small intestine and trophozoites migrate to the large intestine.

  • There, they can cause asymptomatic colonization and produce cysts which are shed with faeces, spreading infection.

  • Amoebic colitis causes "flask shaped" ulcers.

  • Symtoms

    • have subacute onset and
    • can range from mild diarrhoea to severe dysentery.

  • PCR is available and can identify the species.

  • Serology will not distinguish between past infection and acute infection. (Therefore, serology is not not very helpful in endemic areas)

  • PCR is the preferred test > failing which stool antigen -> Stool microscopy (which is the least sensitive)

Patients without symptoms and signs

  • For symptomatic infections: metronidazole or tinidazole.

Low PH is a poor prognostic marker.

  • 90% of ingested PCM is conjugated by UDP-glucuronosyl transferase and other enzymes to water soluble products which are excreted in the urine.
  • The rest is metabolized by the CYP450 system into NAPQI which is toxic. NAPQI is conjugated with glutathione to make it non toxic and is then excreted.
    Most patients are initially asymptomatic; but some of them can go on to develop liver injury.
  • Hepatic and organ injury develops about 10 hours after ingestion -> Antidotes must be started within 8 hours of ingestion.
  • Transaminases elevate by 24 hours.
  • If transaminases > 1000, significant risk of liver damage.
