Kaposi sarcoma

  • Kaposi sarcoma (KS) is an angioproliferative disorder that requires infection with human herpesvirus 8 (HHV-8), also known as Kaposi sarcoma-associated herpesvirus (KSHV), for its development.
  • HHV-8 genome contain viral oncogenes

  • These same genes may cause the endothelial cells to form new blood vessels and may also increase the production of certain chemicals that cause inflammation

  • Most infections with KSHV are asymptomatic. But Immunosuppresion is a risk factor for Kaposir sarcoma development.
  • KSHV is also found in saliva, semen, and vaginal fluid Source

Initially, incorrectly, thought to be a myelopathy.

  • CMT-1: Pain and temperature are usually not affected because they are carried by unmyelinated "C-Fibers".

  • An unstable type of myelin is formed and repeated myelination and demyelination lead to thickened nerves.

  • CMT-2

  • CMT-3: severe, infantile form

  • CMT-4:

  • Onset usually in first two decades of life.

  • Lower limbs more involved than upper limbs; frequent tripping and sprained ankles; steppage gait due to foot drop.

  • Intrinsic foot muscle weakness can cause pes cavus. Muscle wasting -> stork leg or inverted champagne bottle appearance.

  • Upper limb involvement causes difficulty handling small objects like zippers and buttons.

  • There is sensory loss (numbness) as well (although patients may not recognize it because it is chronic)

  • Disease progression appears to accelerate after age of 50 years.

  • Usual laboratory rests are all normal; EMG and high resolution ultrasound may help diagnosis.

  • Slowly progressive disease; lifespan not affected but disability from peripheral muscle weakness will occur. Deformity may need surgical correction. No curative medical therapies exist.

Tay Sach's disease

#2020BSQ-JUL/Q29
A #autosomal-Recessive defect in hexosaminidase which causes accumulation of lipids within cells. Particularly affects CNS neurons. Affected children develop normally until about 6 months of age and then show cognitive decline with loss of milestones, dementia, blindness and chery red spots in the eyes.
There is no treatment.

  1. [[2022 May Basic Sciences#Chronic granulomatous disease|Chronic Granulomatous disease]] <- a defect in neutrophil killing.
  2. Leukocyte adhesion deficiency -> a defect in 'firm' adhesion of leukocytes to vessel walls caused by defect in integrins. #autosomal-Recessive
    • Presents in early life with delayed umbilical cord separation, failure to lose primary dentition, severe gingivitis. There is neutrophilia but the cells cannot reach sites of infection. Recurrent infections in the first decade of life. Treatment is stem cell transplant.
  3. Hyper IgE syndrome - Job's syndrome
    • #autosomalDominant
    • abnormal T cell function, increased IgE production and intermittent defects in neutrophil chemotaxis.
    • Dermatitis, boils, pneumonia
  4. Schwachman-Diamond syndrome
    • Mild neutropenia + defect in neutrophil migration.
    • Resembles cystic fibrosis clinically with exocrine pancreatic failure + pyogenic infections.
  5. Chediak-Higashi syndrome
    • Defective phagolysosom fusion. (large lysosome vesicles are seen within the neutrophils)
    • Recurrent infections, anaemia, hepatomegaly.
    • One cause of primary haemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis.
