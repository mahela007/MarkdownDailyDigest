The problem at hand

[!INFO] TLDR; go paperless!
Have a look at the table below if you need convincing

Post graduate exams are hard. They required candidates to consume and retain vast amounts of information in short period of time, ready for instant recollection come exam day. Case in point: The selection examination for the MD Medicine program conducted by the PGIM of the University of Colombo. The exam is based on a multiple choice format with no essay component. You either know the answer or you don't. (with negative marks for incorrect answers - thrown in for that added rush of adrenaline). Many candidates sit this exam while working as doctors in hospitals around the country, making free time to study a scarce commodity. With a particularly brilliant stroke of genius, I decided that I too would attempt to surmount this towering obstacle. (why? Don't ask)

It was clear from the outset that I would need a good set of notes to prepare. But how to make said notes? If history was anything to go by, I was not a great note taker by any means. To make matters worse, the exam has no defined curriculum. Candidates must peruse about half a decades worth of 'past papers' to glean a rough idea of what the examiners may demand at the next exam. Therefore, any notes I made would have to be made in the order in which I studied the questions and not in 'logical' order of medical topics. This meant that handwritten notes were bound to become untidy as more and more information was crammed into the limited real-estate on a page. These considerations alone made it clear that there was only one way to go: Paperless!

Now, typing out notes instead of writing them is an unusual - and somewhat uncomfortable - approach for many students of medical sciences where dissemination of information has traditionally been paper based(at least locally). While I don't want to be presumptuous enough as to decry hand written notes, allow me illustrate a few advantages of going paperless, for those in doubt.

A bit of meta: Why I am writing this article

It's my hope that at least a few people will find this article useful and use it as an introduction so that paperless note taking and studying will gain traction among the medical student community, paving the way for more systematic collaboration and group study as well as more productive study time.

In a broader sense, I've noticed that many students in the medical community have to surmount a significant knowledge gap when it comes to computing and it's use in everyday life; particularly studying. With this article, I want to show that studying with a PC has far more potential than just 'googling for answers' and I hope that a few seeds of curiosity and enthusiasm will be sown, leading to more adoption and familiarity with digital media and computing.

[!INFO] A word of caution about time management!
Don't get into this with your exams coming up next month!

If you're just making the switch to personal paperless note taking, please make sure that you aren't doing it just before an exam. It takes time to get accustomed to a new 'workflow'; If you're thinking about converting to paperless, give yourself at least -oh I don't know - 5 months or so to become fluent in the new system while studying.
