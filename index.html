Carbon monoxide toxicity

#2023BSQ Q23
CO binds extremely avidly, displacing oxygen from Hb and forming carbon-monoxy haemoglobin => carboxyhaemoglobin.
The dissociation curve of the remaining Hb is also shifted to the left
So

  1. Total amount of Hb availabe to carry O2 is low.
  2. Affinity of remaining Hb for O2 is high -> poor dissociation

Therefore, a person with anaemia will have less symptoms than a person with CO poisoning and the same level of functional Hb.

Severity of poisoning depends on duration of exposure and concentration of CO.
Treatment is with hyperbaric oxygen.
Arterial PO2 remains NORMAL!* ==> No stimulation of respiration despite low oxygen delivery to tissues -> ? pobably no respiratory alkalosis
CO is toxic to tissues but this only occurs at unrealistically high concentrations -> no effect in real life.

Post streptococcal glomerulonephritis PSGN

#2023BSQ Q24
[[Nephrology miscellaneous#Overview of renal histology and function]]

Pathophysiology:
Overview:
Autoimmune response to nephritogenic GABHS -> Immune complexes formed and deposited in glomeruli -> activation of alternate complement pathway -> recruitment of inflammatory cells -> glomerular injury.

Details:
It's not known whether immune complexes are formed in situ at glomeruli or formed elsewhere and deposited.

Histology

  • involvement of the complement pathway is evidenced by finding C3 and IgG with IF stains. They have a granular appearance producing a starry sky pattern.
  • They are components of the "immune complexes".
  • If we use electron microscopy, we see that the immune complexes are dome shaped sub-epithelial deposits (aka "humps"). These are CHARACTERISTIC of PSGN. Subendothelial humps are also seen.

[!INFO]

  • Subepithelial humps -> epithelial damage and proteinuria.
  • Subendothelial humps -> complement activation -> inflammatory cell activation -> proliferative glomerulonephritis -> active urine sediment

en d othelial = sedim en t (there's no d in proteinuria)
epithelial = proteinuria

Deposits on the endothelial side will cause damage on the endothelial side whereas deposits on the epithelial side will cause damage on the epithelial side.

Normal presentation

Latent period : 1 - 3 weeks for GAS pharyngitis, 3 - 6 weeks for skin infection.

The clinical presentation varies from asymptomatic, microscopic hematuria to the full-blown acute nephritic syndrome, characterized by

  1. red to brown urine,
  2. proteinuria (which can reach the nephrotic range - but that's uncommon),
  3. edema,
  4. hypertension,
  5. and an elevation in serum creatinine

[!TIP] The main difference between Nephritic Vs. Nephrotic is not just the level of proteinuria

Investigations
C3 levels / activity is low in the first two weeks due to consumption.
C4 usually normal.

Complications
Hypertensive encephalopathy
Pulmonary oedema
Hyperkalemia
Heart failure

Biopsy
Requirement for dialysis is unusual -> if so, may need biopys.
If C3 is normal on presentation or doesn't rise after 6 - 8 weeks -> unusual -> may need biopsy.
Crescent formation -> poor prognosis

Lupus is a chronic, autoimmune disease that can damage any part of the body

Types of Lupus

  • Cutaneous lupus (skin lupus) is lupus that affects the skin in the form of a rash or lesions. This type of lupus can occur on any part of the body, but usually appears where the skin is exposed to sunlight. - only 5% develop SLE.
    • Three types of skin lupus exist:
      • chronic cutaneous lupus erythematosus (CCLE) (aka Discoid Lupus Erythematosus - DLE) [[Dermatology#Q 10 Discoid lupus erythematosus]]
      • subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus (SCLE),
      • and tumid lupus
  • Drug-induced lupus is similar to SLE, but due to reaction to some medications. Symptoms usually occur 3 to 6 months after starting a medication, and disappear once the medicine is stopped.
  • Neonatal lupus occurs when an infant passively acquires auto-antibodies from a mother with SLE. The skin, liver, and blood problems resolve by 6 months. Can cause congenital heart block -> mortality of 20% and required a pace maker
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

SLE introduction

Autoimmune disease which causes damage to many if not all organs.
Medical treatment can improve quality of life. (only 46% of working age SLE patients remain employed)
Most SLE patients are females of child bearing age so treatment can be complicated.
SLE effects can vary from mild to life threatening.
