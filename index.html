Cat Scratch disease

  • Caused by Bartonella henselae
  • A common cause of chronic lymphadenopathy (several weeks)
  • A cause of PUO.
  • Initial innoculation site develops a papule which lasts 1 to 3 weeks.
  • Followed by lymphadenopathy of the draining nodes; single node involvment is common.
  • Perinaud oculoglandular syndrome is a complication: Facial region lyphadenopathy and infection of conjuctiva, eye lid and adjacent skin.
  • Can also cause neuroretinitis and neurologic manifestations. (encephalopathy)
  • Diagnosis:
    • Mainstay is the clinical picture.
    • Serology is unreliable;
    • Biopsy is not usually required by shows Granulomas. Warthin-Starry stain is used to identify the bacterial
    • PCR on biopsy samples can be done.
    • Culture - fastidious organism; needs specific culture techniques -> inform the microbiology team beforehand.

Mixed connective tissue disease (MCTD)

[!TIP] Mnemonic : SMS
Overlap of SLE, Myositis and Scleroderma

  • AKA Sharp's syndrome.
  • Combines feature of SLE, Systemic Sclerosis and myositis.
  • NOT the same as undifferentiated connective tissue disease.
  • 1/3 undergo remission, 1/3 chronic symptoms, 1/3 severe systemic disease and premature death.
  • Clinical features Source
    • Raynaud's - usually the first symptom
    • Arthritis / arthralgia
    • Oesophageal dysmotility
    • Lung Fibrosis + pulmonary hypertension
    • Myositis
    • High level of anti-U1-RNP
    • presence of anti-snRNP
    • and dsDNA and anti-Scl70 (SLE and SSc) should be negative.
  • Treatment: Similar to that of SLE and SSc.
    • CCBs for Raynauds'.
    • Endothelin receptor agonist / prostacyclin analogues for pulmonary hypertension.

[!INFO] ⬇GFR -> increases urate; conversely gout can impair renal function.
Two main mechanisms : nephrolithiasis and chronic urate nephropathy;
So management of gout with concurrent renal impairment is a clinical issue.

[!INFO] Main aspects of treatment are

  • Analgesia / acute relief
  • Urare Lowering Therapy (ULT) as needed.
    • New guidelines advocate ULT for all patients after the very first episode.
  • There are many medications to use if these fail.

Treatment of acute flares:

  • NSAIDS, colchicine and systemic steroids are all effective.
  • So, choice is determined by contraindications.
  • Colchicine - alternative to NSAIDS. Inhibits neutrophil microtubules, thereby impairing neutrophil migration into sites of inflammation but it has slower onset of action and also contraindicated (UpToDate) (or⬇dose (BNF)) in renal impairment.
  • Corticosteroids - oral prednisolone or IM or intra-articular depot methylprednisolone.

Colchicine

  • use with caution in combination with P450 inhibitors.
  • GI side effects are the commonest.
  • Can cause neuromyopathy -> numbness and weakness with chronic therapy.
  • First few episodes of gout are managed symptomatically as above. (new guidelines recomment ULT from first episode)
    • Non purine analog that inhibits xanthine oxidase.
    • Probenecid - avoid in renal impairment

    • Anakinra - IL-1 inhibitor; very expensive.
