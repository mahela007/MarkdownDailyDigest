Trypanosomiasis - sleeping sickness

#insect-vector
Pathogen : protozoan - trypanosoma sp.
Vector : Tsetse fly - Glossina
Enedemic to subsaharan africa
About a week after the bite, the patient develops a chancre at the site of the bite.
Other syptoms are lymphadenopathy (painless, posterior chain) and hepatosplenomegaly.
The parasite moves through the lympathic system can can invade the CNS --> late stage symptoms involve CNS dysfunction (aka. sleeping sickness)
Usually a slowly progressing disease; many are diagnosed incidentally.

Treatment: nifurtimox-eflornithine, fexinidazole, pentamidine

Legionella pneumonia

Legionella pneumophila is a thin, aerobic, pleomorphic, flagellated, non-spore-forming, Gram-negative bacterium of the genus Legionella

Pneumonia is the commonest manifestation of legionella infection. Infection of other sites is rare.
Clinically and radiologically indistinguishable from other pneumonias
Can cause mild to severe disease.
Suspect if pneumonia is resistant to beta lactam treatment

  • Associated hyponatremia
  • CRP > 100;
  • elevation of liver enzymes
  • GI symptoms
  • Exposure (from contaminated water in buildings)

Diagnosis: PCR of respiratory secretion but if this is not possible, urinary antigen can be checked.

Mortality can be up to 10%.
Treatment: fluoquinolone or macrolide.

Levofloxacin and azithromycin are the preferred agents for the treatment of Legionnaires' disease because these agents are bactericidal, achieve high intracellular concentrations, penetrate lung tissue, and are active against all Legionella species that cause human infection. Alternatives include other fluoroquinolones (eg, moxifloxacin, ciprofloxacin) and other [[Antibiotics#Why clarithromycin? Why is it interchangeable with metronidazole|macrolides]] (eg, clarithromycin, roxithromycin)

Mycoplasma pneumonia

Mycoplasma is one of the smallest known free living organisms.
No cells wall -> beta lactams won't work.

Commonest manifestation is upper respiratory tract infection.
but mycoplasma pneumonia is a common cause of community acquired pneumonia as well.
Most mycoplasma associated URTI and LRTI are mild and self limiting; They may not even need ABx. Pursuing a microbiological diaganosis usually isn't necessary as management won't change.

Clinically and radiologically, mycoplasma pneumonia has no distinguishing features except

  • Haemolytic anaemia - typically mild and self limiting [[Haematology MCQ discussion#Q 7 Mycoplasma and haemolystic anaemia|Haemolytic Anaemia in mycoplasma infection]]
  • presense of upper respiratory symptoms (rhinorhea, ear ache)

Diagnosis: PCR

Directed treatment of M. pneumonia For patients with microbiologically confirmed M. pneumoniae pneumonia, first-line treatment options include macrolides (eg, azithromycin), tetracyclines (eg, doxycycline), and respiratory fluoroquinolones (eg, levofloxacin or moxifloxacin).

  • Fever (prolonged; about 2 weeks if untreated), headache, fatigue
  • may have splenomegaly
    #insect-vector
    Transmitted via larval tick bite. (trombiculid mites)
    Pathogen: Bacterium - Orientia tsutsugamushi - intracellular pathogen, belongs to ricketsia

Other causes of elevated creatinine:
+ High intake of cooked meats, creatine supplements, high intensity excercise (muscle breakdown ==> creatinine formation)
+ Some drugs: NAC, cephalosporin, haemolysis
+ H2 blockers (cimetidine, ranitidine)

Theoretically, clearance should be measured using a substance which is freely filtered, not secreted and not reabsorbed. The ideal substance is inulin.
In practice creatinine is used because it is an endogenous substance. But creatinine is secreted in small amounts by the tubules (15% is secreted). Therefore, using creatinine as the molecule slightly overestimates the GFR.
Accurately measuring creatinine clearance would require measuring urine creatinine. So for convenience, we measure the serum creatinine and approximate the GFR based on that.

This is done using fancy formulas such as MDRD formula or the newer CKD-EPI formula which account for age, sex, race etc when producing an eGFR value. Source

Reabsorption of filtered solutes

K+

Mostly reabsorbed in PCT (actively by Na/K ATPase) . Secreted in DCT and CD
Secretion not rigidly coupled to Na+ but is influenced by luminal charge.

Therefore, less Na in DCT -> Less Na reabosorbed -> less luminal 'negativity' created -> less K+ secreted.
DCT has K/H (antiport) ATPase -> increased H+ secretion -> increased K+ resorption (therefore decr. K+ excretion)

Aldosterone acts on distal DCT and CD causing

  • Incr. ENAC -> Incr. Na permeability
  • Incr. basolateral Na/K ATPase activity (Na out of cell, K+ into cell)
  • Both effects contribute to Incr. Na reabsorption and increased K+ secretion into the tubule.

Water reabsorption

In the PCT

Aquaporin 1 - PCT
PCT is permeable to water so tubular fluid remains isotonic as water follows solutes being removed.
60-70% of water is reabsorbed in the PCT.

In the collecting duct:

Tubular fluid entering the collecting duct is hypotonic because solutes have been removed by the TAL and DCT.
As filtrate passes along the CD, water is reabsorbed (enabled and regulated by Vasopressin action) as the CD passes into progressively hypertonic environment of the renal medulla. So --> lots of vasopressin--> aquaporin 2 --> concentrated urine created from dilute tubular fluid entering the collecting duct.

Note how the percentage of reabsorbed fluid changes only by 1% but urine output dops by 2L! (due to 180L/day of GFR)

Medullary osmolality depends on availability of urea. urea is produced by protein metabolism. Low protein diet -> low urea -> impaired concentrating ability
