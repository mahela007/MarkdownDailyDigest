[!INFO] Lichen planus: described by 4 P's
Slowly (and spontaneously) resolving T cell mediated autoimmune disease

  • Cause is unknown.
  • Typical lesions have a Violaceous / lilac colour.
  • intensely itchy!
  • Flat topped lesions.
  • Wickham's striae are seen in older people.
  • Wickhams striae: white lace - like patterns on the surface of lesions. - seen in most but not all
    • Cutaneous wickhams is seen in all patients.
    • Oral wickhams striae is seen only in 50% of patients.
  • Affects skin,mucous membranes (especially the oral mucosa), scalp, nails, and genitalia - see UpToDate images
    Pathogenesis: - possible autoimmune based involving CD 8 T cells
    Histology:
  • Hyperkeratosis without parakeratosis
  • Small clefts at the dermal-epidermal junction (Max-Joseph spaces)
  • Band-like, lymphocytic infiltrate at the dermal-epidermal junction
  • Eosinophilic, colloid bodies (apoptotic keratinocytes) in the papillary dermis
  • Pigment incontinence (most prominent in dark-skinned individuals)
    Management:
  • Cutaneous lichen planus usually resolves within 2 years.
  • Treatment is focused on decreasing time to resolution and managing pruritus.
  • Local steroid, systemic steroids are the mainstay although not backed by good evidence.
  • Retinoids may be used.
  • + other drugs
    Differential diagnosis:
  • lichenoid reaction to certain drugs

[!INFO] Thiazides
Thiazides are one drug class than can cause lichenoid reaction.

Clostridium tetani is a gram positive rod. It will not grow in healthy tissues.

  • Tetanospasmin is an exotoxin produced by the bacteria.
  • It is a potent neurotoxin.
    It enters peripheral motor nerves and travels to the CNS where it inhibits GABA (which is itself inhibitory). There is subsequent loss of inhibition of the anterior horn cells -> painful muscle spasm.
  • Loss of inhibition of autonomic nerves: Severe autonomic instability; sweating, tachycardia and hypertension.
  • The effects on the neurons are long lasting because recovery requires the growth of new axonal nerve terminals
    Tetanus has a very variable incubation period; upto 21 days! PEP should be given if patient presents up to 21 days after exposure.
  • C tetani is sensitive to penicillin and metronidazole.
  • Mainstay is Benzodiazepine and MgSO4.
  • Tetanus does not induce immunity. So all patient should get active and passive immunization following tetanus infection.

[!INFO] How to remember this nonsense
Skin disorders of malignancy:

  • Erythema gyratum repens - lung cancer - wood grain appearance, forests are the lungs of the world
  • Acanthosis nigricans - gastric ca - always goes along with fat/tummy related things
  • Acquired ichthyosis - lymphoma - lymfishma
  • Acquired hypertrichosis lanuginosa - GI and lung cancer - hairy man who smokes and drinks
  • Dermatomyositis - ovarian and lung ca - always think of this one as paired with myositis, these are paired organs
  • Migratory thrombophlebitis - pancreatic cancer - are scorpion stings also prothrombotic? Let’s imagine yes
  • Necrolytic migratory erythema - glucagonoma - always carry some glucose whilst migrating
  • Pyoderma gangrenosum - myeloproliferative disorders - pyo/myo
  • Sweet syndrome - haematological malignancies - sweets increase blood sugar
  • Tylosis - oesophageal cancer - tie a tie around your oesophagus

Bacterial toxins

  • Endotoxins - released only when the cells are lysed

  • Exotoxins - secreted by the bacterial cells.

  • Exotoxins are generally released by gram positive bacteria.

    • Notable exceptions (i.e gram -ves) are vibrio cholerae and some species of E. coli.
