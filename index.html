Randomization in clinical trials

The aim is to minimize variation in sample size and variation in baseline characteristics (covariates) between treatment and control groups.

Randomization:

Randomization is the process of assigning participants to treatment and control groups, assuming that each participant has an equal chance of being assigned to any group

Randomization can use a method as simple as a coin toss but better methods are available.

Benefits of randomization:

  1. Eliminate influence of covariates on the dependent variable when comparing control and treatment groups.
  2. Eliminates selection bias (i.e eliminates the change of individuals having unequal chances of being assigned to the study groups)
  3. ANCOVA and multivariate ANCOVA are techniques used to adjust for covariate imbalance between the groups during the analysis stage.

Techniques of randomization

  1. Simple randomization : complete randomness => flipping a coin.
    1. Good for large trials but will produce uneven groups in small trials (n< 100)
  2. Block randomization
    1. Ensures equal sample sizes between groups
    2. but may introduce unevenness in covariates.
    3. Makes groups comparable within known confounding factors.
  3. Stratified randomization
    1. Achieves balance among covariate characteristics between groups.
  4. Adaptive covariate randomization
    1. "covariate adaptive randomization method allows for the examination of previous participant group assignments to make a case-by-case decision on group assignment for each individual who enrolls in the study"

[!INFO] Blinding
In a single blind study - particicipants are blinded.

  • Minimizes performace bias (i.e subjects will perform differently when they have knowledge of their treatment / control group)
    In a double blind study - participants and researchers are blinded.
  • double blinding also minimizes assessment bias (when researches assess a participants different when they have knowledge about whether subject belongs to the the control / treatment group)

Types of error

 - Type 1 error (where the null hypothesis is incorrectly rejected)
 - Type 2 error (where the null hypothesis is incorrectly accepted)

Covariates (to understand randomization)

Covariates are any variable that is statistically related to the dependent variable in a study. (i.e changing a covariate will affect the dependent variable in question).
Covaraites can be

  1. The independent variable being studies
  2. Confounding variables.
    Covariates are continuous variables. So as opposed to measuring the effect of 'treatment or no treatment' on a dependent variable, using covariates allows us to to check the effect of a continuous variable on the dependent variable.
  • Linear regression is one method of analysing the effect of a covariate on the dependent variable; it's used when the dependent variable is also continuous.

if the covariate accounts for a reasonable amount variation in the dependent variable, we increase statistical power to examine effects of a factor that interests us; again, this provides clear benefits. Conversely, including a covariate that does not explain reasonable variation in the dependent variable decreases power to examine effects of a factor. Source
