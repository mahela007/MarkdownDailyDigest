Ventilation and perfusion of the lung

[!INFO] Ventilation and perfusion are BOTH greater at the bases
However, increase in blood (from top to bottom) is greater than the increase in ventilation (from top to bottom).
Therefore, overall, V/Q ratio decreases from top to bottom.

$$
\frac{V}{Q}{top} > \frac{\large{↑V}}{\Large{↑↑Q}}{bottom}
$$

In a healthy individual, the V/Q ratio is 1 at the middle of the lung, with a minimal spread of V/Q ratios from 0.3 to 2.1 from base to apex. Source

Ventilation:
Less negative intrapleural pressure at the base -> lower transmural difference at the base -> Basal alveoli are less expanded -> Therefore, they are more compliant -> Therefore, large change in volume during inspiration -> bases have higher ventilation.

Perfusion:
Perfusion is also greater at the bases.

[!INFO] These are usually attributed to gravity
because these variations decrease in the supine position.
However, they are still present in weightlessness of space.

Possible effect of V/Q ratio on alveolar PO2

In cases of high V/Q ratios, PO2 increases and PCO2 decreases as alveolar air more closely matches the larger volume of inspired air than perfused blood. On the other hand, low V/Q ratios result in a decreased PO2 and an increased PCO2. Source
So ? alveolar PO2 would be higher at the top and lower at the bottom of the lung.

Pulmonary vascular resistance

Pulmonary mean arterial pressure = 15mmHg.
Mean pulmonary venous pressure (= LAP = wedge pressure) = 5mmHg.

[!INFO] How to rember:
PA = alveolar;
Pa - arterial, Pv - venous
The position of PA progressively moves to the right side of the comparison; it becomes lower and lower relative to the vascular pressures.

The lower the alveolar pressures, the less distended they are -> less pressure on the vessels -> less vascular resistance.
Vascular resistance is lowest in zone 3 and HIGHEST IN ZONE 1 Source

Effect of anatomic dead space

Rapid breathing at low tidal volume and slow breathing at large tidal volume both result in the same minute ventilation.
But because of anatomical dead space, with rapid shallow breathing, less air reaches the alveoli resulting in significantly less alveolar ventilation.

Cholinergic crisis

Cholinergic hyperstimulation secondary to inhibition of acetylcholinesterase enzyme.
Symptoms: hypersalivation, lacrimation, muscle weakness, paralysis, blurry vision due to both nitocinic and muscarinic toxicity.
